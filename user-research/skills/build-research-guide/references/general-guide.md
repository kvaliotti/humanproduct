# General Research Guide Methodology

Synthesized from The Mom Test (Fitzpatrick), Deploy Empathy (Hansen), and Just Enough Research (Hall). Use this reference when building a research guide for discovery, validation, evaluative, or exploratory research — any study where the primary goal is understanding users, problems, or markets rather than changing a specific behavior.

## 1. Core Principles

Every question in the guide must pass three tests (The Mom Test):

1. **Talk about their life instead of your idea.** If the question reveals your idea, it invites people to protect your feelings.
2. **Ask about specifics in the past instead of generics or opinions about the future.** Past behavior is fact. Future promises are fiction.
3. **Talk less and listen more.** If you're talking more than they are, you're doing it wrong.

Hall's framing: "Your questions are just prompts to help the participant tell you a story that reveals situations, attitudes, and behaviors you didn't even think to ask about." The guide exists to get out of your way, not to constrain the conversation.

Hansen's framing: The script is your scaffolding — not your cage.

---

## 2. Guide Structure Overview

Every guide has these sections regardless of interview type. The content within each section adapts to the interview type (see Section 11 for templates).

| Section | Timing | Purpose |
|---------|--------|---------|
| Opening | 2-3 min | Establish safety, set expectations, frame the conversation |
| Broad Context | 2-5 min | Confirm the problem area matters before zooming in |
| Problem Exploration | 5-15 min | Map current reality through behavioral stories |
| Digging on Signals | Throughout | Go deeper when emotion, requests, or interesting behavior surfaces |
| Reaching for the Door | Halfway mark | The most important question — opens the floodgates |
| Commitment & Advancement | When appropriate | Push for concrete next steps (not during pure discovery) |
| Closing | 2-3 min | Referrals, wrap-up, admin |

## 3. Section 1: Opening — Frame the Conversation

### VFWPA Framework (The Mom Test)

Use this to open without triggering social desirability bias:

- **Vision:** Half-sentence on how you're making the world better. Don't mention your product.
- **Framing:** Where you're at and what you're looking for. "We're just starting out and don't have anything to sell."
- **Weakness:** Show how they can help. "I'm having a hard time understanding how X works from the [their role]'s perspective."
- **Pedestal:** Show why them specifically. "You've been doing this for years and could really help me cut through the fog."
- **Ask:** Ask for help, not for their time.

### Opening Script (Deploy Empathy)

```
Hi, is this [person]? Thank you for taking the time to talk to me today.
I'm so excited to talk to you. Is this still a good time to talk?

Before we get started, I just wanted to ask if you had any questions for me?

[If offering incentive:]
I also just want to check if it's okay I send you a [$X gift card]
after we get off the phone here?

[If recording:]
Oh, just before we get started, is it okay if I record this interview?
It's just so I can listen to what you're saying and don't have to be
scribbling notes the whole time. It won't be shared outside our organization.
```

### Additional Opening for Interactive/Screen Share Interviews

```
I want to just make sure it's super clear that we're testing the site, not you.
You can't do anything wrong. If something doesn't make sense to you, there's a
good chance it wouldn't make sense to someone else. So your honesty is super helpful.

As you're going through the site, please narrate out loud everything you think
as you think it. Remember, you can't offend me.
```

### Additional Opening for Cancellation Interviews

```
I appreciate your generosity in helping us understand what went wrong so we can
prevent other people from having the disappointing experience you've had.
Before we get started, I just want to say explicitly that this isn't a sales call,
and nothing you say here will be used for sales purposes.
```

### Opening Rules
- Keep it casual. If it feels like an interview, you've over-formalized it.
- Avoid: "Thanks for agreeing to this interview. I just have a few questions."
- Good: Natural conversation tone, interest in their world.
- Bad: Clipboard energy, scripted transitions, scales of 1–5.

### Study Description (Just Enough Research)
Include 1-2 sentences to share with the participant. General enough to set scope, specific enough to be useful, vague enough not to influence answers.
- Good: "We're interested in how you select and plan activities for your family."
- Bad: "We want you to tell us what would entice you to visit the Fantastic Science Center."

## 4. Section 2: Broad Context — Do They Care?

Start broad. Don't zoom into your problem area until they raise it organically or you've confirmed it matters.

**Questions to establish whether the problem area matters at all:**
- "What are your big goals and focuses right now?"
- "What are the biggest problems you're dealing with?"
- "What are the top 3 things you're trying to fix or improve right now?"
- "How seriously do you take [area]?"
- "Do you make/spend money on this?"

**Why this matters:** If you zoom prematurely into your problem, you'll get detailed answers to a question that doesn't matter. Someone's #1 fitness problem is still irrelevant if fitness isn't in their top 10 priorities.

**When to skip this section:** Only when you are 100% certain the problem is a must-solve. Even then, validate for each new sub-segment.

## 5. Section 3: Problem Exploration — Their Life, Their Words

Once you've confirmed the area matters, explore their current reality.

### Essential Questions (The Mom Test)

| Question | What It Gets You |
|----------|-----------------|
| "Talk me through the last time that happened." | Concrete workflow data, real behavior, not hypothetical |
| "How are you dealing with it now?" | Current alternatives, price anchor, workaround sophistication |
| "What else have you tried?" | Whether they're actively searching (if not, they won't buy yours) |
| "Why do you bother?" | Root motivation — gets from perceived problem to real one |
| "What are the implications of that?" | Whether the problem is worth paying to solve, or just annoying |
| "Where does the money come from?" | (B2B) Budget ownership, purchasing process, hidden stakeholders |
| "Talk me through your workflow." | How your product fits into their day, integration requirements |

### Question Bank by Dimension (Deploy Empathy)

**Process questions (functional):**
- What are all the steps involved in [this process]?
- What manual steps are involved?
- What tools do you use for this?
- How long have you been using those tools?
- How did you decide to switch to those tools?
- Are there tools you tried that didn't work well?
- What happens before/after you do [thing with our product]?

**Frequency and pain questions (willingness-to-pay signals):**
- How often do you find yourself doing this?
- How long does [particular step] take you?
- Can I ask how much you pay for [that tool]? And is that monthly, yearly, as you use it...?
- If you didn't do this [task], what would happen?
- Thinking about the whole process, what takes the most time?

**Social questions:**
- How many team members are involved with [process]?
- Is there anyone outside your team involved?
- Is there anyone outside your company involved?
- Was there anyone else who needed to weigh in before you could use [product]?
- Did you talk to anyone else before making the purchase?

**Emotional questions (ask indirectly — never say "feel"):**
- What were you hoping [product] would do for you?
- Before you started using [product], was there anything you were unsure about?
- What is your least favorite part about [this process]?
- What led you to start looking for something new?

### Context Questions (Just Enough Research)
- "Walk me through a typical day/week."
- "How often are you online?"
- "What devices do you use? When? Do you share any?"
- "What do you typically do on your days off?"
- "How do you decide what to do?"

### Anchoring Questions (use when they give generics or fluff)
- "When's the last time that happened?"
- "Can you talk me through how you handled it?"
- "Can you give me a specific example?"

## 6. Section 4: Digging on Signals

When you hit a signal (strong emotion, feature request, interesting behavior), dig.

### For Feature Requests (The Mom Test)
- "Why do you want that?"
- "What would that let you do?"
- "How are you coping without it?"

**Rule:** Ideas and feature requests should be understood, but not obeyed. Dig for the motivation, not the specification.

### For Emotional Signals
- "Tell me more about that."
- "That seems to really bug you — what's the story?"
- "What makes it so awful?"
- "Why haven't you been able to fix this already?"
- "You seem pretty excited about that — it's a big deal?"

### Magic Wand Question (Deploy Empathy — use late in the interview)
"A bit of a silly question, so bear with me. If you could change any part of this [process] with a magic wand, what would it be?"

### Follow-up Probes (Just Enough Research)
- "Why is that?"
- "Tell me more about that."
- "Can you give me a specific example?"

### Clarification Probes (Deploy Empathy)
"Can I just make sure I understand? First you do X, then Y, then Z. Does that sound right?"
**Level-up:** Intentionally restate something slightly wrong. They'll correct you with much more detail.

## 7. Section 5: Commitment & Advancement

Only include this section when you have something concrete (product, prototype, clear next step). Don't push for commitment during pure problem-discovery conversations.

### Commitment Currencies (The Mom Test)

| Currency | Examples |
|----------|----------|
| **Time** | Clear next meeting with goals, trial for a non-trivial period, feedback session on wireframes |
| **Reputation** | Intro to peers/team, intro to decision maker, public testimonial |
| **Money** | Letter of intent, pre-order, deposit |

**How to push:** Don't ask "What do you think?" Ask for a specific next step.
- "Would you be willing to trial this with your team for two weeks?"
- "Can you introduce me to [budget holder]?"
- "Can I come back next week to show this to the rest of the team?"

## 8. Section 6: Reaching for the Door (Deploy Empathy)

This is the most important question in the entire interview. Ask it at the halfway mark to leave the entire second half open for what follows.

```
Thank you so much for taking the time to talk to me today.
I've learned a lot from you. [breath]
Is there anything else you think I should know?
```

**Then WAIT.** Do not prompt. Do not suggest topics. Just wait at least 3 beats.

If they say "No" or "Not really" — wait longer. 9 out of 10 times there's a pause, and they'll fill it.

If truly nothing comes, try variants:
- "Is there anything else you want to add?"
- "Is there anything you hoped we'd talk about?"
- "Were there any questions or thoughts you had for me?"

**Why this works:** The first half of the interview primes them to think about the topic and establishes trust. The second half is where the floodgates open. The scripted questions build rapport; the unscripted second half produces the deepest insights.

---

## 9. Section 7: Closing

**Always ask:**
- "Who else should I talk to?" (Generates warm intros — your most valuable asset)
- "Is there anything else I should have asked?" (Lets them fix your line of questioning)

**Admin wrap-up (Deploy Empathy):**
```
[If offering incentive:]
Is it okay if I go ahead and send you that gift card now?
Can you confirm that [email] is the right place to send it to?

[If the interview went well:]
I've really enjoyed our conversation. If we end up building something
that tries to solve this, can I reach back out to you and get your thoughts?
```

## 10. The 13 Conversational Rules (Deploy Empathy)

These are NOT optional. The script is scaffolding; these rules are the load-bearing structure. Include the top 5-7 most relevant to the specific guide on the first page as a visible reminder.

1. **Use a gentle tone of voice.** Picture asking a treasured older family member about a photo of themselves as a young person. Use that voice. Genuinely curious. Soft.

2. **Validate them.** Use validating statements — not agreement, not praise, not opinions.
   - "That makes sense."
   - "I can see why you'd do it that way."
   - "It sounds like that's frustrating/time consuming/challenging."
   - Use "think" not "feel": "You think the process is complicated" > "You feel the process is complicated"

3. **Leave pauses for them to fill.** Ask the question. Wait at least 3 beats. Do NOT prompt with possible answers. Do NOT rephrase. Let silence work. If they ask "Are you still there?" — "Yes, I was just giving you a moment to think."

4. **Mirror and summarize their words.**
   - Mirror: Repeat what they said. "You had ten calls today."
   - Summarize: Rephrase as a statement. "It sounds like you had a lot of calls today, and because someone didn't show up, you're feeling frustrated."
   - Use "It sounds like..." not "I'm hearing that..." — center the situation, not yourself.

5. **Don't interrupt.** The interviewer should do ~10% of the talking.

6. **Use simple wording.** "Can you give me the big picture of what you're trying to do?" > "What are your objectives?" Imperfect grammar is fine. Reduce cognitive load.

7. **Ask for clarification, even when you don't need it.** "Can I just make sure I understand? First you do X, then Y, then Z. Does that sound right?"

8. **Don't explain anything or get defensive.** When someone asks "Why does it work like this?" respond with: "Can you walk me through what you expected to happen?"

9. **Build on what they say — never negate.** Treat it like improv: "Yes, and..." Never "Well, but..." or "Actually..."

10. **Let them be the expert.** If they say something factually wrong, do NOT correct them. Everyone is the expert of their own experience.

11. **Use their words and pronunciation.** If they say "MailMonkey" instead of "MailChimp," you say "MailMonkey." Correcting shatters trust.

12. **Ask about past or current behavior, never predictions.** Never "Would you use this?" Always "What do you currently use?"

13. **Be a rubber duck.** Don't propose solutions. Don't share ideas. Don't fix. Just listen and reflect back.

## 11. Interview Type Templates

Match the guide structure to the interview type specified in the brief.

### Discovery
Opening → Broad context (do they care?) → Process questions → Tools/alternatives → Frequency/pain → Social (stakeholders) → Magic wand → Reaching for the door → Closing

### Switch
Opening → "How did you get to needing this?" → Previous solutions → Point of struggle → When they started looking → How they found you → Decision process → Social (other decision-makers) → Satisfaction → Reaching for the door → Closing

### Long-time Customer
Opening → "Big picture of how you use [product]" → What they used before → What happens before/after your product → How things have changed → "If you couldn't use us, what would you do?" → Feature requests (with process probing) → Magic wand → Reaching for the door → Closing

### Cancellation
Opening (with explicit "not a sales call") → Deal with disappointment first → Other contributing situations → Decision to cancel → Why they came in the first place → What they'll use next → Reaching for the door → Closing

### Interactive / Usability
Opening (with "testing the site, not you") → Acclimating → First impressions → Expectations ("If you clicked that, what would you expect?") → Task analysis (repeated loops) → Decision process → Purchase process → Magic wand → Reaching for the door → Closing

### Card Sorting
Opening → Acclimate to the board → "Is anything missing?" → Sort by importance → Sort by satisfaction → Probe gaps (important + low satisfaction) → Reaching for the door → Closing

## 12. Questions to Never Ask (or Never Trust the Answer)

| Bad Question | Why It Fails | Better Alternative |
|-------------|-------------|-------------------|
| "Do you think it's a good idea?" | Invites opinion and approval | Ask about their life; decide yourself |
| "Would you buy a product that did X?" | Hypothetical; answer is always "yes" | "How do you currently solve X? How much does it cost you?" |
| "How much would you pay for X?" | Feels rigorous but is equally hypothetical | "How much do you currently pay to solve it? What's the budget?" |
| "Would you ever...?" | Everyone "might" someday | "When's the last time you did...?" |
| "Do you usually...?" | Generic claims about identity, not behavior | "What did you do last Tuesday?" |
| "What are your pain points?" | Jargon; invites performed suffering | "Talk me through the last time [X] happened." |
| "What do you need?" | They can't want what they can't imagine | "How are you dealing with it now?" |
| "What are you struggling with?" | Offensive to many | "What takes the most time?" |

## 13. Conversational Tactics (The Mom Test)

Include the most relevant 3-4 as reminders at the top of the guide.

### Deflect Compliments
When they say "That's cool, I love it" — ignore it. Redirect: "Thanks — but how are you dealing with this stuff at the moment?"

### Anchor Fluff
When they say "I always/never/usually" — get specific: "When's the last time that happened? Talk me through it."

### Cut Off Your Own Pitch
When you catch yourself monologuing: "Whoops — I just slipped into pitch mode. I'm sorry about that. Can we jump back to what you were saying?"

### Shut Up
After asking a question, wait. Silence is a tool. Let them fill it.

## 14. Note-Taking System

### Symbols for Quick Capture (The Mom Test)

| Symbol | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| :) :( :\| | Excited / Angry / Embarrassed |
| ☇ | Pain or problem |
| ⨅ | Goal or job-to-be-done |
| ☐ | Obstacle |
| ⤴ | Workaround |
| ^ | Background or context |
| ☑ | Feature request or purchasing criteria |
| ＄ | Money, budgets, purchasing process |
| ♀ | Mentioned a specific person or company |
| ☆ | Follow-up task |

Write exact quotes in quotation marks. You'll use them for marketing language, fundraising decks, and resolving team debates.

### Documentation Requirements (Just Enough Research)
- Notetaker working from a copy of the guide with space for annotations
- Key items to note: nonverbal frustration, verbatim quotes, features that succeeded or failed, vocabulary used
- Audio recording (always, with consent) — "people have crappy memories"
- Video recording only when the extra information justifies the overhead
- Time codes (even approximate) — makes analysis dramatically easier
- Between-session check: verify files are saved correctly, named consistently, initial impressions noted while fresh

## 15. Logistics & Formatting

### Script Formatting Tips (Deploy Empathy)
- Print the script with 4-6 line breaks between questions (space for notes)
- Print single-sided (flip for freeform diagrams)
- Keep 4-5 questions per page
- Write a few validating statements at the top as a "phrase bank" in case you panic
- Have a blank paper and pencil for diagramming processes

### Session Logistics
- Duration: Early broad questions = 5–15 min. Detailed workflow = 15–30 min. Industry deep-dive = 60+ min.
- Team: 2 people ideal (one talks, one takes notes). Never more than 2 unless it's group-on-group.
- Format: In-person > Phone/Video. Casual > Formal. Coffee > Boardroom.
- Founders must attend. You cannot outsource customer learning.

### Conducting Checklist (Just Enough Research / Helsinki Design Lab)
- [ ] Create a welcoming atmosphere.
- [ ] Always listen more than you speak.
- [ ] Take responsibility to accurately convey participants' thoughts and behaviors.
- [ ] Conduct research in the natural context of the topic.
- [ ] Start with a general goal description — don't focus responses too narrowly.
- [ ] Encourage participants to share thoughts and go about their business.
- [ ] Avoid leading and closed yes/no questions. Ask follow-ups.
- [ ] Prepare questions in advance but don't be afraid to stray.
- [ ] Snap photos of interesting things and behaviors.
- [ ] Note exact phrases and vocabulary participants use.
- [ ] Pay attention after you stop recording — revelations happen then.

## 16. Usability Test Additions (Just Enough Research)

When the guide is for usability testing, add these components:

### Task Scenarios
For each feature to test, write a brief story that offers background on how the user arrived and what they're trying to accomplish.

**Example:** "A science-minded school friend is in town for the weekend and wants to visit the Fantastic Science Center. You did a web search and landed on this page. What would you do to find and purchase tickets for the weekend your friend is visiting?"

**Task design rules:**
- Prioritize tasks by importance — know which failures are deal-breakers
- Make tasks realistic and grounded in persona goals
- Don't reveal the "correct" path in the task description

### Test Plan Checklist
Include alongside the guide:
- Objectives
- Subject of the test (what you're testing, what state it's in)
- Methodology
- Participants and recruiting
- Procedure
- Tasks
- Usability goals: completion rate (% of tasks user can complete), error-free rate (% completed without errors)

### Interviewer Conduct for Usability
- **Don't help.** If they struggle, resist the urge to intervene. That's the data.
- **Don't lead.** Avoid inserting opinions, especially during rapport-building when you're relaxed.
- **Breathe.** Tension is contagious. Relaxed participant = better data.

## 17. Contextual Inquiry Additions (Just Enough Research)

When observing in the field, structure the guide around:

1. **Travel logistics** — allow plenty of time, know the setup
2. **Getting situated** — find a comfortable spot that doesn't interrupt their routine
3. **Interview phase** — establish trust, learn what you'll observe, ask when it's OK to interrupt
4. **Observation phase** — note everything in detail, pause to ask questions, stay out of the way
5. **Summary phase** — conclude by summarizing what you learned, ask participant to verify
