# General Research Approach

Synthesized from The Mom Test (Fitzpatrick), Deploy Empathy (Hansen), and Just Enough Research (Hall). Use this reference for discovery, validation, evaluative, and exploratory research — any study where the primary goal is understanding users, problems, or markets rather than changing a specific behavior.

## 1. Framing the Research Question

The research question is the anchor. Everything in the brief serves it.

**Deploy Empathy rule:** Tie the question to a specific business decision. If you can't name the decision this research will change, the question isn't sharp enough.

**Just Enough Research rule:** Use an outcome verb — describe, evaluate, identify, analyze. Avoid "understand" or "explore" (you'll never know when you're finished).

**Formula:** "We will [outcome verb] [topic] [by method]."

| Weak | Strong |
|------|--------|
| "What do users think?" | "Why aren't more people buying from this landing page?" |
| "What should we build?" | "Should we launch a new product for [given problem]?" |
| "Are customers happy?" | "What use cases keep our top-revenue customers renewing?" |
| "Understand our users" | "Identify the top 3 reasons free trial users don't convert to paid" |

**Sub-questions:** If the main question has components, list them. Each should be clear and falsifiable.

## 2. Stating Current Beliefs

From The Mom Test: write down your best guesses before talking to anyone. These are targets for disconfirmation, not plans to defend.

| Element | What to write |
|---------|---------------|
| **Customer hypothesis** | Who specifically has this problem? (Demographics + behaviors, not just demographics) |
| **Problem hypothesis** | What is the problem, and why does it matter enough to pay/act? |
| **Solution hypothesis** | How do you believe your product solves it? |
| **Existing alternatives** | What do they do today instead? What are they paying (money, time, effort)? |

Being explicit about beliefs is what makes them testable. Unwritten assumptions can't be challenged.

## 3. The Big 3 Questions

From The Mom Test: every batch of conversations needs exactly 3 learning goals — the murkiest or most important unknowns right now.

**Selection rules:**
- Each question must have the power to destroy or reshape the business. If an unexpected answer wouldn't change what you do, it's not important enough.
- At least one question should scare you. If none feel uncomfortable, you're avoiding the elephant in the room.
- They should be answerable through conversation. If desk research can answer it, do that first.
- They evolve. After each batch, retire answered questions and promote the next-murkiest ones.

**Bad questions** (trivia, not business-critical):
> "What apps do they currently use?" / "How old are our users?"

**Good questions** (could kill the idea):
> "Are they actively spending money to solve this, or just complaining?"
> "Does the budget holder even know this problem exists?"
> "Can we actually reach these people, or are they unfindable?"

**Pre-mortem prompts to surface hidden risks:**
- "If this company were to fail, why would it have happened?"
- "What would have to be true for this to be a huge success?"

## 4. Risk Mapping

### Market vs Product Risk (The Mom Test)

| Risk type | Core question | Conversational value |
|-----------|---------------|----------------------|
| **Market risk** | Do they want it? Will they pay? Are there enough of them? | High — conversations are your primary tool |
| **Product risk** | Can I build it? Can I grow it? Will they keep using it? | Lower — conversations give a starting point, but you'll need to build earlier |

If risk is mostly product risk (games, marketplaces needing critical mass), acknowledge that conversations alone won't validate the business.

### Risk Justification (Just Enough Research)

Answer: Given our stated business goals, what bad thing happens if, six months from now, we realize:
- We are solving the wrong problem
- We were wrong about organizational support
- We don't have the competitive advantage we assumed
- We built features that excited us but don't matter to customers
- We failed to reflect what is actually most important to users
- Users don't understand the labels we're using
- We missed a key aspect of users' environments
- We were wrong about prospective users' habits and preferences

If there is no risk associated with an assumption, don't spend time investigating it. Rank by risk. Focus on highest-priority.

## 5. Research Type Classification

From Just Enough Research and Deploy Empathy:

| Type | Driving Question | When to Use |
|------|------------------|-------------|
| **Generative / Exploratory** | "What's up with...?" | Before you know the problem. Looking for ideas. |
| **Descriptive / Explanatory** | "What and how?" | Problem defined, need context homework. |
| **Evaluative** | "Are we getting close?" | Have a proposed solution, need to test it. |
| **Causal** | "Why is this happening?" | Product is live, observed behavior needs explanation. |

From Deploy Empathy — scope by timeline:

| Scope | When | Timeline |
|-------|------|----------|
| **Project-based** | Specific question to answer | 1-2 months alongside other work |
| **Ongoing** | Building general customer understanding | Continuous, integrated into workflow |

## 6. Customer Segmentation

### The Mom Test: Customer Slicing

A segment is a who-where pair. If you can't say where to find them, keep slicing.

**Process:**
1. Start with a broad group
2. Within this group, who would want it most?
3. Would everyone buy, or only some? Why?
4. What are they already doing to solve this?
5. Where can I find people doing that workaround?
6. Repeat until you have a findable, specific sub-group

**Selection criteria:**
- **Profitable** — they have budget and motivation
- **Reachable** — you know where to find them
- **Rewarding** — you want to build a business around them

### Deploy Empathy: Behavioral Segmentation

Define participants by behavior, not demographics:
- **Time-based:** Signed up within last 30 days, customer for 1+ year, canceled last quarter
- **Revenue-based:** Specific plan, LTV threshold, free trial non-converters, upgraders
- **Behavior-based:** Used feature X, never used feature Y, high-frequency users

### Just Enough Research: Screening

A good research participant:
- Shares the concerns and goals of your target users
- Embodies key characteristics (role, context, familiarity level)
- Can articulate their thoughts clearly
- Is as familiar with relevant technology as your target users

**Screener design:**
- Screen for behaviors first — they matter more than demographics
- Be vague about actual test contents (prevents gaming)
- Include decoy questions to mask true screening criteria
- Keep screener short to reduce drop-off

## 7. Interview Type Selection

From Deploy Empathy:

| Interview Type | Use When | Key Goals |
|----------------|----------|-----------|
| **Discovery** | Exploring a new idea; understanding if a problem exists | Validate problem existence, frequency, pain, current solutions, stakeholders |
| **Switch** | Understanding why someone became a customer | Map the journey from awareness to purchase; find acquisition insights |
| **Long-time customer** | Understanding retention drivers | Find why happy customers stay; uncover well-served use cases |
| **Cancellation** | Understanding churn | Find what attracted wrong-fit customers; prevent future churn at the source |
| **Interactive** | Testing a prototype, landing page, or live product | Evaluate usability and value; uncover decision-process blockers |
| **Card sorting** | Prioritizing problems or features | Understand which problems are most important and least well-served |

## 8. Core Questions Framework

From Deploy Empathy — every research brief should map back to these:

1. What are they trying to do overall? (Goal)
2. What are all the steps in that process? (Process)
3. Where are they now in that process? (Current state)
4. Where does the problem you're solving fit? (Product fit)
5. Where do they spend a lot of time or money? (Pain signals)
6. How often do they experience this? (Frequency)
7. What have they already tried? (Existing solutions)

These are structural questions — adapt the specific phrasing to your research context.

## 9. Participants & Recruitment

### Sample Size (Deploy Empathy)
- **Minimum:** 5 people per discrete question (surfaces ~80% of needs per Nielsen/Landauer)
- **Complex problems:** 10-15 people
- **Stop rule:** When you start hearing the same things repeatedly
- **Research loops:** If 5 interviews yield wildly different answers, narrow the scope and do another round of 5

### Diversity
Include people with disabilities, different demographics, different organizational contexts. Leaving out groups means leaving out opportunities.

### Recruitment Copy Formula (Deploy Empathy)
1. Who you are
2. A specific amount of time you need
3. Why you want to talk to them
4. When you want to talk
5. Incentive (if applicable)

**Rules:**
- Keep it short — one sentence per point, with line breaks
- Zero promotional language
- Ask about recent, concrete behavior, not hypothetical future behavior
- LinkedIn: personalize heavily, research them first
- Email to existing customers: use your title, mention that feedback influences the roadmap

### Incentives (Deploy Empathy)
- Discovery: often not needed
- Reddit/cold outreach: $10-25 gift card
- Cancellation: always needed ($25)
- Screen share: $10-25
- Existing happy customers: swag

## 10. Logistics

| Element | Guidance |
|---------|----------|
| **Format** | Audio-only preferred (people open up more without video); screen share only for interactive interviews |
| **Duration** | Start with 30 minutes; extend to 45-60 if needed; 20-25 min for cancellation interviews |
| **Recording** | Always ask permission; use Otter.ai or equivalent for transcription |
| **Interviews per day** | Maximum 2; 1 per day or per week is also fine |
| **Note capture** | Print script with space between questions, or type in a doc; have blank paper for diagrams |
| **Data storage** | Naming convention: "Study-SubjectName-YYYY-MM-DD", shared drive, backup plan |
| **Fallback plans** | If recruiting delays: push dates, relax criteria, or talk to fewer people now and more later |

## 11. Roles

From Just Enough Research — assign every role explicitly. One person can hold multiple roles, but each role must have an owner.

| Role | Responsibility |
|------|---------------|
| **Point Person** | Owns the plan and the checklist — prevents things from falling through cracks |
| **Author** | Plans and writes the study (problem statement, questions, interview guide) |
| **Interviewer / Moderator** | Interacts directly with participants |
| **Coordinator / Scheduler** | Plans time allocation, schedules sessions |
| **Notetaker / Recorder** | Captures data during sessions |
| **Recruiter** | Screens potential participants, identifies good subjects |
| **Analyst** | Reviews data for patterns and insights (should be more than one person) |
| **Documenter** | Reports findings post-study |
| **Observer** | Watches research in progress (clients, stakeholders) |

## 12. Ethics & Bias

### Ethical Safeguards (Just Enough Research)
- Is the overall project ethical? Will its success lead to harm?
- Informed consent: participants must understand goals, how data will be recorded/used/shared
- Safety and privacy: ensure participant comfort, no fatigue, no risks from your presence
- Recording permissions: explicit consent before recording
- Never conduct phone interviews while anyone involved is driving

### Bias Acknowledgment (Just Enough Research)

| Bias Type | What It Is | Mitigation |
|-----------|-----------|------------|
| **Design bias** | Study structure shaped by personal goals/preferences | Peer review of study design |
| **Sampling bias** | Sample doesn't represent target population | Note limitations in conclusions |
| **Interviewer bias** | Inserting opinions during interviews | Practice interviews, internal critique |
| **Sponsor bias** | Participant deference to the commissioning org | Use general descriptions, don't name company until necessary |
| **Social desirability bias** | Participants giving "good" answers instead of true ones | Emphasize honesty, promise confidentiality |
| **Hawthorne effect** | Behavior changes because participants know they're observed | Blend in, encourage normal behavior |

## 13. Analysis Approach

Decide upfront how you'll analyze results:

- **Customer journey map:** Diagram steps, tools, functional/social/emotional elements at each step
- **Pain and frequency matrix:** 2x2 grid (pain x frequency); high-pain + high-frequency = highest willingness to pay
- **Quick analysis:** Pull immediate actions (marketing copy, testimonials, landing page ideas) directly from customer quotes
- **Team review (Stripe model):** Pull notes → extract themes with quotes → sit on themes → brainstorm actions → share with leadership

---

## 14. Success Criteria

Define what "done" looks like before starting. Examples:
- "We can identify the top 3 reasons people cancel and the acquisition source that attracts them"
- "We can diagram the end-to-end process and identify which steps take the most time"
- "We have enough signal to decide whether to build Feature X or invest in marketing Feature Y"

---

## 15. Commitment & Advancement

From The Mom Test — know what "success" looks like at the end of each conversation:

| Stage | Success = |
|-------|-----------|
| **Early (problem discovery)** | Concrete facts about their life, not compliments. No commitment needed yet. |
| **Mid (solution validation)** | A commitment of time, reputation, or money (intro to boss, agreement to trial, letter of intent) |
| **Late (sales)** | Advancement to next step in your funnel (demo with team, pre-order, deposit) |

**Desk Research First (The Mom Test):** Before talking to anyone, answer what Google can answer. Spend 5 minutes on LinkedIn and the company website before any meeting. Don't waste conversation time on things you could have looked up.
