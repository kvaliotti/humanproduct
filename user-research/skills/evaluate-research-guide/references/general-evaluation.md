# General Research Guide Evaluation Reference

Synthesized from The Mom Test (Fitzpatrick), Deploy Empathy (Hansen), and Just Enough Research (Hall).

Use this reference when evaluating guides for discovery, validation, evaluative, or exploratory research — any study where the primary goal is understanding users, validating problems, or informing decisions (rather than driving a specific behavior change).

---

## Section A: Mom Test Compliance

Run every question in the guide through this filter. A guide that fails Mom Test compliance will collect compliments and fluff instead of facts.

| Criterion | Pass | Fail |
|---|---|---|
| About their life, not your idea? | "How do you currently handle X?" | "What do you think of our approach to X?" |
| About specifics in the past? | "Talk me through the last time this happened" | "Would you ever use a product that...?" |
| Avoids leading the witness? | Open-ended, doesn't suggest the answer | "Don't you hate when X?" / "Wouldn't it be great if Y?" |
| Could even their mom not lie about it? | Asks about concrete actions already taken | Asks for opinion, prediction, or approval |
| Interviewer talks less? | Question is brief; space is left for their answer | Question is a paragraph followed by prompting |

### Automatic Fail — These Questions Should Never Appear

- "Do you think it's a good idea?"
- "Would you buy a product that did X?"
- "How much would you pay for X?"
- "Do you like it?"
- Any question beginning with "Would you ever...", "Could you see yourself...", "Do you think you..."
- "What do you want?" / "What features would you want?" (without follow-up probing motivation)
- "What are your pain points?" (vague, different meaning to different people)
- "What are you struggling with?" (offensive framing — people don't like being told they struggle)
- "Are you confused by..." (implies fault on participant)
- Any "Why did you..." in accusatory framing

### Replacement Patterns

| Forbidden Pattern | Replace With |
|---|---|
| "Would you use this?" | "What do you currently use for [X]?" |
| "Would you pay for this?" | "Can I ask how much you pay for [that tool]?" |
| "What are your pain points?" | "How long does [step] take you?" / "What tools do you use?" |
| "What features do you want?" | "Can you walk me through the last time you needed to do [X]?" |
| "What are you struggling with?" | "What was it like to get started with [X]?" / "Compared to what you expected, how long did it take?" |
| "Why did you...?" (accusatory) | "What led you to..." / "Can you walk me through..." |

## Section B: Structure

### B1. Opening / Act 1

| Check | Pass | Fail |
|---|---|---|
| Avoids mentioning product/idea | Frames around their world, their problem area | Describes the product in the first minute |
| Avoids the word "interview" | "I'd love to learn about how you handle X" | "Thanks for agreeing to this interview" |
| Uses VFWPA or equivalent framing | Vision, framing, weakness, pedestal, ask | No framing — jumps straight into questions |
| Sets right power dynamic | You're asking for help; they're the expert | You're pitching; they're evaluating |
| Study description included | 1-2 sentences, general enough not to bias | Sponsor identity or product name revealed |
| Administrative items covered | Purpose, data usage, recording permission | Consent missing |
| Warm-up feels natural | Built from demographic info, eases tension | Interrogation-style demographics block |

### B2. Body / Act 2

| Check | Pass | Fail |
|---|---|---|
| Starts broad before zooming | "What are your big priorities right now?" | Jumps directly into feature-level questions |
| Gate before zoom | "If they mention [area], continue; if not, explore why" | Fixed sequence regardless of relevance |
| Questions organized by topic, not rigid sequence | Guide is a checklist, not a script | Numbered questions read verbatim in order |
| Follow-up / probing prompts included | "Why is that?" / "Tell me more" / "Can you give a specific example?" | No follow-up prompts |
| Priority/hierarchy visible | Interviewer knows essential vs. nice-to-have | All questions appear equally important |
| Room for improvisation | Space for unexpected topics | Every minute accounted for |

### B3. Closing / Act 3

| Check | Pass | Fail |
|---|---|---|
| Asks "Who else should I talk to?" | Present — generates warm intros | Missing — dead end |
| Asks "Is there anything else I should have asked?" | Present — surfaces blind spots | Missing |
| Thank-you and admin | Incentives, next steps covered | Abrupt ending |
| Early exit protocol | Interviewer knows how to end unproductive sessions | No plan for graceful exit |

### The Premature Zoom Trap

If the guide asks "What's your biggest problem with [your area]?" before establishing that [your area] is even in their top 10 concerns, every answer will be misleading. The person will invent an answer because you asked, not because they care.

## Section C: Question Quality

### C1. Seven Core Questions Coverage (Deploy Empathy)

Every guide should probe all 7, directly or indirectly:

| # | Core Question | Covered? |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | What are they trying to do overall? | |
| 2 | What are the steps in that process? | |
| 3 | Where are they now? | |
| 4 | Where does the problem fit in their process? | |
| 5 | Where do they spend time or money? | |
| 6 | How often do they experience this? | |
| 7 | What have they already tried? | |

NEEDS WORK if any core question is missing and the interview type doesn't justify its absence.

### C2. Three-Dimensional Probing

| Dimension | What to Probe | Sample Questions |
|---|---|---|
| **Functional** | Steps, tools, time, money, manual processes | "What tools do you use?" / "How long does that take?" |
| **Emotional** | Hopes, frustrations, uncertainties, satisfaction | "What were you hoping it would do?" / "Was there anything you were unsure about?" |
| **Social** | Other decision-makers, team dynamics, stakeholders | "Was there anyone else who needed to weigh in?" / "Who else is involved?" |

FAIL if only functional questions are present. Emotional and social dimensions drive willingness to pay and switching behavior.

### C3. Behavior vs. Prediction Orientation

All questions must ask about past or current behavior, facts, and concrete experiences. Any question asking participants to predict future behavior or express hypothetical intent is a fail.

| Type | Example |
|---|---|
| Behavior (keep) | "Can you tell me about the last time you did [X]?" |
| Prediction (remove) | "If we built [feature], would you use it?" |
| Behavior (keep) | "How much do you pay for [tool]?" |
| Prediction (remove) | "How much would you pay for [our product]?" |

### C4. Open-Ended vs. Closed

| Quality | Indicator |
|---|---|
| Open-ended (keep) | "Tell me about...", "Walk me through...", "How do you..." |
| Closed (rewrite) | "Do you...?" / "Would you...?" / yes-no questions |
| Story-eliciting (keep) | Invites narratives, not single-word answers |
| Indirect (keep) | Real question behind the asked question — reveals through stories |

### C5. Tone and Wording

PASS if:
- Questions use simple, conversational language (not corporate or academic)
- Grammar is intentionally imperfect to put people at ease
- Questions use softeners: "Can you tell me..." / "I'm curious..." / "I'm wondering..."
- Indirect questions used for sensitive topics
- No jargon unless certain the participant uses the same jargon

NEEDS WORK if:
- Multiple questions bundled into one
- Questions are grammatically perfect in a way that increases cognitive load

## Section D: Dig Triggers and Follow-Up Readiness

### D1. Dig Triggers

| Signal | Guide Should Include |
|---|---|
| Strong emotion | "If they show strong emotion → dig with 'Tell me more'" |
| Feature request | "If feature request → ask 'Why? What would that let you do?'" |
| Fluff/generics | "If 'always/never/usually' → anchor with 'When's the last time?'" |
| Vague answer | "Can you say more about that?" / Mirror what they said / Summarize |

FAIL if guide moves linearly with no branching or dig prompts.

### D2. Follow-Up Phrase Bank

PASS if script includes space between questions for notes AND suggested follow-up tactics:
- "Can you say more about that?"
- "Mhmm"
- [Mirror what they said]
- [Summarize what they said]
- "It sounds like..."
- [Silence]

### D3. Reaching for the Door Question

The question "Is there anything else you think I should know?" should appear at the **halfway mark**, not the end. The second half should be open for follow-up on what surfaces.

FAIL if: This question is missing or placed at the very end.

## Section E: Commitment and Advancement (When Appropriate)

| Research Stage | What the Guide Should Include | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|
| **Problem discovery** | No commitment push needed; success = concrete facts | Pushing for sales before understanding the problem |
| **Solution validation** | Specific commitment to ask for (trial, intro, deposit) | Ends with "What do you think?" |
| **Sales** | Clear advancement step (demo with team, pre-order) | Ends with compliment-fishing |

---

## Section F: Interview Type Match (Deploy Empathy)

Check that the guide matches the stated interview type:

| Type | Must Include | Must NOT Include |
|---|---|---|
| Discovery | Problem exploration, alternatives tried, frequency, stakeholders | Product demos, selling, feature lists |
| Switch | Timeline from awareness → struggle → switch → satisfaction | Future product plans |
| Long-time customer | Big picture use, feature requests (with process probing), magic wand | Leading questions about features you want to build |
| Cancellation | Disappointment acknowledgment, "not a sales call," what they'll use next | Any attempt to save/retain the customer |
| Interactive/Usability | "Testing site, not you," narrate-aloud instruction, expectation questions | Hints, prompts, or rescue when they struggle |
| Card sorting | Board acclimation, importance and satisfaction ranking, probe gaps | Pre-sorted or pre-ranked items |

## Section G: Usability Test Tasks (If Applicable)

| Check | What to Look For |
|---|---|
| Tasks framed as scenarios | Brief story providing context for why the user is there |
| Tasks are realistic | Based on actual persona goals, not features the team wants validated |
| Task priority ranked | Team knows which failures are deal-breakers vs. minor |
| No path revealed | Task description doesn't hint at the "correct" solution |
| Metrics defined | Completion rate, error-free rate, severity/frequency scales ready |
| Facilitator ≠ designer | Designer will unconsciously help; if unavoidable, document bias |

---

## Section H: Bias Prevention

| Check | Pass | Fail |
|---|---|---|
| Sponsor identity concealed | General framing ("how you plan activities") | Specific framing ("what you think about our product") |
| No enthusiasm leaking | Warm-up doesn't telegraph interviewer's passion | Interviewer excitement visible in framing |
| Neutral language throughout | No loaded words, no implied "right" answers | Leading or emotionally charged language |
| Probing prompts neutral | "Tell me more" | "That sounds frustrating, right?" |
| Practice session planned | Guide tested internally before real participants | First use is with a real participant |

---

## Section I: Documentation and Capture Plan

| Check | What to Look For |
|---|---|
| Notetaker role assigned | Separate from interviewer |
| Note template provided | Copy of guide with annotation space |
| Key capture items specified | Nonverbal signals, verbatim quotes, vocabulary |
| Recording plan clear | Audio at minimum; consent process defined |
| Time-coding planned | Even approximate timestamps for later analysis |
| Between-session protocol | File naming, initial impressions captured while fresh |

---

## Section J: Debugging Preparedness

| Scenario | Guide Should Include |
|---|---|
| No-show | Re-scheduling template ready; reminders sent |
| Interview too short | Mirror/summarize tactics listed; "Can you say more?" prompts |
| Nervous participant | Extra validating statements prepared |
| Short/curt answers | "From the way you're responding, I'm getting the sense you might be in a time crunch. Would another time be better?" |
| Cagey participant | "What made you interested in talking to me today?" |
| Feature request takeover | "Can you walk me through a scenario where you'd use that?" |
| Expected onboarding | Backup onboarding script prepared; consider rescheduling interview for later |
| Multiple people on call | "I'm interested to hear more about what led you to want to jump on today." |
| Off-topic tangent | "Thank you for telling me that. I'm wondering if we could go back to something you mentioned earlier." |
| Angry participant | Listen first. Solve their problem, then understand the source. |

NEEDS WORK if no contingency planning at all.

---

## Section K: Contextual Inquiry Readiness (If Applicable)

If the research includes in-person observation or contextual inquiry, evaluate these additional dimensions:

| Check | What to Look For |
|---|---|
| Travel logistics planned | Enough time to arrive and set up |
| Observation protocol defined | Where to position, when to ask questions, how to stay out of the way |
| Summary/verification step included | End by summarizing observations, asking participant to verify |
| Environment documentation planned | Photos, notes on physical context, tools in use, interruptions |

---

## Section L: Practical Usability of the Guide Itself

| Check | Pass | Fail |
|---|---|---|
| Fits on a few pages | Interviewer can glance at it | Binder-length document |
| Questions are scannable | Not buried in paragraphs | Dense text blocks |
| Checkboxes or tracking mechanism | Can mark off topics during conversation | No tracking |
| Printable and shareable | Works on paper and tablet | Requires special tools |

---

## Section M: Brief Alignment

| Check | What to Look For |
|---|---|
| Research questions covered | Every research question from the brief has at least one corresponding question in the guide |
| Scope discipline | Guide stays focused on what the brief specified — no drift into adjacent topics, satisfaction surveys, or feature wishlists |
| Participant match | Guide is written for the participant segment defined in the brief |

### The Ultimate Test

> "If this participant tells me everything this guide prompts me to ask about, will I have the information I need to make the design decisions identified in the research brief?"

If no — the guide has gaps. If yes — the guide is ready.

## Anti-Pattern Checklist

| Anti-Pattern | How to Spot It | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| **The script** | Rigid sequence, numbered questions, no branching | Rewrite as conversation prompts with dig triggers. Add "If X, then Y" branches |
| **Pitch disguised as research** | Product description appears before questions. Questions lead toward "isn't our solution great?" | Remove all product references from the first 80% of the guide |
| **Comfort zone** | No question feels risky. All safe and comfortable | Add the scary question from the research brief. Add a commitment push |
| **Feature-shopping** | "Would you use feature A? Feature B? Feature C?" | Replace with "How do you currently handle [workflow]?" and dig |
| **Scale-itis** | "On a scale of 1-5, how important is..." | Replace with "Tell me about the last time this mattered" |
| **No escape hatch** | Doesn't account for the person not caring about the problem | Add early broad questions; plan for graceful exit |
| **Solo-optimised** | No note-taking plan, no signal symbols, no review process | Add note-taking protocol and post-conversation review step |
| **Satisfaction interview in disguise** | Opens with "How do you feel about...", majority opinion-based | Restructure to open with behavioral reconstruction |

## Bad Data Detector

### Compliment Generators
Questions that expose your ego or seek approval:
- "So here's what we're building — what do you think?"
- Any question where "That's awesome, I love it!" is a plausible response

### Fluff Generators
Questions that invite generic, hypothetical, or future-tense answers:
- "Do you usually...?" → identity claim, not behavior
- "Would you ever...?" → always gets "maybe/yes"
- "How much would you pay?" → optimistic fantasy number

### Idea Collectors (Without Motivation Digging)
Questions that accept feature requests at face value:
- "What features would you want?" (without: "Why? How do you cope without it?")
- "What would your dream product do?" (without: "What's behind that? What's the real goal?")

## Quick Litmus Test

1. **If I ran this guide with my mom, could she lie to me?** If yes, the questions aren't concrete enough.
2. **If the conversation went perfectly, would I learn facts or collect compliments?** If compliments, rewrite.
3. **If I got an unexpected answer, would I know what to do next?** If not, add branching and dig triggers.
4. **Could someone leave this conversation thinking I was just interested in their life?** If not, you're pitching.
