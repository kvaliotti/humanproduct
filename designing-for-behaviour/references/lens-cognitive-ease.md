# Lens: Cognitive Ease & Decision Design

**Distilled from:** *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (Kahneman). Kahneman's own chapters overlap heavily; this lens keeps only the decision-and-perception contribution and drops the sections that duplicate the loop lens (his "engagement audit" / "adoption audit" are covered by **behavioural-loop** and are not re-scored here).

## What this lens sees

Users decide with **System 1** — fast, automatic, effort-avoiding, story-making — and only reluctantly engage **System 2**. At every choice point the product either makes the desired action the one System 1 takes *without thinking*, or it loses the user to friction, ambiguity, a competing option, or a bias pointed the wrong way. This lens audits the **moment of decision** and the **memory** of the experience — not the loop mechanics (that's behavioural-loop) — asking: *is the valuable path also the fluent, obvious, low-effort, low-risk, well-framed path?*

## 1. System-1 fit at the decision point  *(behaviour)*

- At each key moment, is the desired action **visually obvious without deliberate search**, and understandable almost instantly? Does the interface support *recognition* over *recall* (nothing to hold in memory)?
- Is the primary action **perceptually dominant**, and do competing actions not visually fight it? Is the default path the desired path?
- Does the interaction match the user's existing mental model, using familiar patterns where novelty would just add effort — and novelty only where attention/learning is the point?
- Can a **distracted, busy, tired** user complete it? The product should assume users are energy-conserving, not attentive.
- Does the product avoid ambiguity at critical points rather than relying on users to "figure it out"?

## 2. Cognitive ease & fluency  *(behaviour, adoption)*

- Is the language **clear rather than clever**? Are labels immediately understandable to *this* user; are important messages short and concrete; is jargon absent at decision points?
- Is there visual/textual strain near important actions (clutter, dense text, poor contrast, tiny targets, unclear icons)? Strain silently pushes users toward "no."
- Can the user **predict what happens after clicking**? Does the product avoid making users feel stupid?
- Is the *clearest* path also the *most valuable* path — or does fluency lead somewhere shallow?

## 3. Effort economy — spend System 2 only where it pays  *(behaviour, adoption)*

- Does the product minimise cognitive load and **not ask for decisions before value is clear**? Does it avoid presenting too many options at once, and use **progressive disclosure** instead of front-loading complexity?
- Are repeated decisions that could be automated actually automated? Are useful defaults, summaries, examples, and recommendations doing the deciding where the user lacks a basis to choose?
- Is friction **reserved for moments where reflection genuinely helps** (destructive/irreversible actions) rather than sprinkled everywhere?
- Can users recover from a minor error without restarting a whole flow?

## 4. Framing  *(behaviour, adoption, engagement)*

Framing changes behaviour without changing the facts. Check whether key asks are framed to support the good action honestly:

- Is the desired action framed around **user gain / progress / avoided loss** deliberately, and does the team know which frame produces the behaviour?
- Is **onboarding framed as progress toward value**, not setup work? Is **upgrade framed as unlocking**, not a tax? Renewal as *retained value*; reactivation as *restarting progress*, not correcting failure?
- Is repeated use framed as *accumulation*, not repetition?
- Does framing **clarify reality rather than distort it** — no misleading percentages, hidden negatives, or opt-out made harder than opt-in? (Cross-check the ethics gate.)

## 5. Anchoring — first numbers, examples, expectations  *(adoption, engagement)*

- Does the product deliberately manage the **first** price/score/benchmark/example the user sees, so it anchors toward value? Does it **anchor value before price, outcomes before features, improvement before mere usage**?
- Does onboarding set an expectation of *continued* use, and does the first completed action become a reference point for future behaviour?
- Does it avoid accidental low anchors — an empty state that anchors inactivity, free usage when paid adoption is the goal, manual work when automation is the goal, shallow use when depth is the goal?

## 6. Defaults & choice architecture  *(behaviour, adoption)*

- Does the **default** support the user's likely best interest *and* the desired behaviour, while staying transparent and easy to change?
- Are there sensible presets and recommended choices where users lack expertise, without burdening novices with customization before value is felt?
- Does the product protect users from accidental destructive actions, and use confirmation only where mistakes matter (not dialogs users learn to ignore)?
- Is the **easiest path the highest-value path**? Choice architecture is treated as a responsibility, not a conversion trick (ethics gate).

## 7. Loss aversion, endowment & status quo  *(adoption)*

Losses loom larger than gains, and people overvalue what they already have — both resist adoption and both can support it honestly:

- Does the product identify what users fear **losing** by adopting/switching, and reduce perceived switching cost (map old→new, make migration/trial feel reversible)?
- Does the user **create/customize/save/earn** something early, so progress feels owned and abandonment feels like leaving value behind? Is past work surfaced at return moments?
- Is there a **safe, reversible first step** before full commitment? Is the cost of the status quo made visible without guilt-tripping?
- Is ownership built from *genuine accumulated value*, not artificial hostage-value/dark patterns (ethics gate)?

## 8. WYSIATI & substitution — are users deciding on the right question?  *(behaviour)*

- **WYSIATI** ("what you see is all there is"): do users act on the incomplete picture the product shows? Is a critical tradeoff hidden behind a persuasive CTA; does one vivid impression (a halo) dominate the whole evaluation? Do users correctly understand the offer, the value, the commitment, and the risk?
- **Substitution**: does the experience make users answer an *easier* question than the real one — "Is this pretty?" instead of "Will this help me?"; "Do I intend to use this?" instead of actually using it?
- Does the **team** substitute, too? NPS standing in for adoption, time-on-page for engagement, clicks for task completion, stated preference for observed behaviour. Flag where the product/measurement is fooling itself.

## 9. Attention & memory of the experience  *(engagement)*

- Is **salience used to guide behaviour**, not decorate — is the most important element the most visually salient? Are alerts/badges/notifications sparing, timed for receptivity, and each tied to a clear next action (not noise that trains users to ignore them)?
- Are paused tasks easy to resume; are unfinished intentions remembered; is cognitive residue from incomplete flows minimised?
- **Peak–end (remembering self):** does the experience create memorable progress moments and **end well**? A single bad final moment contaminates the whole memory and the decision to return. Are onboarding/purchase/support/cancellation endings designed deliberately?
- **Experiencing self:** are the worst moment-to-moment moments (waiting, uncertainty, repetitive effort) improved — not just the average — since people avoid returning to remembered pain?

## Not your lens

Whether a trigger/reward/investment loop exists and compounds → **behavioural-loop**. Whether the user gets genuinely better / achieves outcomes outside the product → **capability-results**. Whether influence stays honest and preserves autonomy → **control-autonomy** + ethics gate (this lens surfaces the *mechanisms* — framing, defaults, loss aversion — that can be used well or manipulatively; the ethics call is made there). Note cross-lens observations in one line; don't score them.
