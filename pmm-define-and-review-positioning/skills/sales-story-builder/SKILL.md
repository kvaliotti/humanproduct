# Sales Story Builder

Translate completed positioning into a 7-stage sales narrative. This is the most important post-positioning step — it forces team alignment on problem definition, current solution gaps, and how positioning translates to a pitch.

## Why Sales Story Before Messaging

The sales story forces agreement on:
- How to define the problem (not your product's features — the customer's situation)
- What current solutions look like and where they fail
- The gap between reality and ideal
- How your positioning fills that gap with proof

This alignment is harder and more valuable than wordsmithing marketing copy. Get the story right, then messaging flows naturally.

## Prerequisites

This skill requires a completed positioning canvas (or equivalent):
- Competitive alternatives
- Unique attributes
- Value themes with proof
- Target market
- Market category
- (Optional) trends

If the user hasn't completed positioning, redirect them to the positioning-workshop or help them fill in the minimum viable canvas first.

## The 7-Stage Sales Story Arc

Build each stage in order. Use `references/story-arc-detail.md` for examples and templates.

### Stage 1: Define the Problem

Frame the customer's world — the situation, the friction, the stakes.

**Rules:**
- Start with the customer's reality, not your product
- Name the specific pain, not a generic industry challenge
- Make it resonate: the prospect should think "that's exactly my situation"

**Template:** "[Target customers] are trying to [goal]. But [specific obstacle] makes this [difficult/expensive/risky]."

### Stage 2: Current Solutions and Their Shortcomings

Show what the prospect is likely doing today (from competitive alternatives) and why it falls short.

**Rules:**
- Reference actual alternatives from the positioning canvas — not straw men
- Be fair: acknowledge what current solutions do well
- Then identify the specific gap — the thing that current solutions can't solve

**Template:** "Most teams today rely on [Alternative A] or [Alternative B]. These work for [what they handle], but they [specific gap 1] and [specific gap 2]."

### Stage 3: The Perfect World

Paint the ideal outcome — knowing what you know about the gaps. This sets up purchase criteria that favor your product.

**Rules:**
- Describe outcomes, not features
- Make it specific enough that prospects can evaluate solutions against it
- The "perfect world" should naturally map to your value themes

**Template:** "Imagine if [target customer] could [ideal outcome 1], [ideal outcome 2], and [ideal outcome 3] — without [current pain]."

### Stage 4: Introduce Your Product in Its Market Category

This is where positioning appears. Position the product in its market category so the prospect immediately has context.

**Rules:**
- State the market category explicitly
- One sentence maximum
- No hype adjectives

**Template:** "[Product] is a [market category] that [one-line description of unique mechanism]."

### Stage 5: Value Themes with Detail

Walk through each value theme from the positioning canvas. For each:
- State the theme
- Explain HOW your product delivers it (the unique attributes)
- Show WHY it matters (benefit → customer goal)
- Map to the "perfect world" vision from Stage 3

**Structure:** One section per value theme (1-4 themes total).

### Stage 6: Proof Points

For each value theme, provide evidence:
- Customer quotes or case studies
- Metrics and measurable outcomes
- Third-party validation
- Demo-able capabilities

Also address likely objections:
- "What about [table-stakes feature]? Do you have it?"
- "How does this compare to [specific alternative]?"
- "Who else uses this?"

### Stage 7: Next Step / Call to Action

What should the prospect do next? Be specific:
- Demo
- Trial
- Workshop
- Technical evaluation
- Reference call

**Rules:**
- One clear next step, not a menu
- Match the CTA to the sales motion (self-serve vs. enterprise)
- Make the CTA low-friction relative to the deal size

## Output

Deliver the sales story in two formats:

**1. Full Sales Story Document** — all 7 stages, written out, ready for a sales team to internalize.

**2. Story-on-a-Page** — condensed version (one paragraph per stage) that fits on a single page for quick reference.

## Downstream: What Comes After the Sales Story

Brief the user on what should follow:

| Area | Impact of New Positioning |
|------|--------------------------|
| **Messaging** | Create a master messaging document. All campaigns start from this baseline. |
| **Product Roadmap** | Feature priorities may shift to align with new market frame. |
| **Pricing** | Price expectations vary by category. Align pricing with the new frame. |
| **Sales** | Target segmentation, demo data, terminology all need updating. |
| **Customer Success** | Onboarding flow and expansion strategy may change. |

Also note: revisit positioning every 6 months or when triggered by new competitors, regulation changes, technology shifts, or economic climate changes. If repositioning is needed, restart at Step 4 (competitive alternatives).

## Style Rules

- The sales story is NOT a feature list. It's a narrative arc: problem → gap → vision → solution → proof → action.
- Every stage must be written from the CUSTOMER's perspective, not the company's.
- Test each stage: "Would a prospect nod along, or glaze over?" If glaze, rewrite.
- Ban all hype adjectives. Everything must be specific and provable.
