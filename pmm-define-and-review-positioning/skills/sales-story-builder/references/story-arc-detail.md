# Sales Story Arc: Detailed Reference

## Stage-by-Stage Guide with Examples

### Stage 1: Define the Problem

**Purpose:** Frame the discussion. Set the boundaries. Make the prospect think "this person understands my world."

**Structure:**
- Describe the customer's situation (industry, role, what they're trying to accomplish)
- Name the specific friction point
- State the stakes (what happens if the problem persists)

**Example (insurance claims management):**
> "Insurance companies are under pressure from digital-native competitors to make the claims experience faster and more transparent. Customers expect to file, track, and resolve claims from their phone. But today, most insurers still require multiple touchpoints — phone calls, paper forms, in-person assessments — creating friction that drives customer churn and increases operational cost."

**Common mistakes:**
- Starting with your product ("We help companies...")
- Being too generic ("Companies need to be more efficient")
- Being too technical ("Legacy systems create API integration challenges")

### Stage 2: Current Solutions and Their Shortcomings

**Purpose:** Show you understand what the prospect is doing today. Build credibility. Then reveal the gap.

**Structure:**
- Name 2-3 actual alternatives (from positioning canvas)
- Acknowledge what they do well
- Identify the specific gap

**Example:**
> "Most insurers have invested in basic mobile functionality — customers can check their policy status or contact an agent through an app. Some have added photo-based damage assessment. These are good first steps, but they still require manual handoffs between systems. A customer starts a claim on mobile, then gets a phone call to verify details, then waits for an email with the assessment. The experience feels digital at the edges but analog at the core."

**Common mistakes:**
- Setting up straw men (unfairly characterizing alternatives)
- Skipping this stage (jumping straight to "here's our product")
- Listing too many alternatives (pick 2-3 most relevant)

### Stage 3: The Perfect World

**Purpose:** Create the mental image of what "good" looks like. Set purchase criteria that favor your product.

**Structure:**
- Describe outcomes, not features
- Make it specific and measurable
- Align with value themes from positioning canvas

**Example:**
> "Imagine if a customer could file a claim, upload photos, get an AI-powered damage assessment, receive an offer, and accept a payout — all within a single mobile session, in under 10 minutes. No phone calls, no waiting, no handoffs. And the insurer gets real-time visibility into every claim, with fraud detection built in."

**Common mistakes:**
- Describing features instead of outcomes
- Being too vague ("a better experience")
- Setting up a perfect world that your product can't actually deliver

### Stage 4: Introduce Product in Category

**Purpose:** Position the product. One sentence. Give the prospect the mental model they need.

**Structure:**
- Product name
- Market category
- One-line unique mechanism

**Example:**
> "ClaimFlow is a mobile claims management platform that automates the end-to-end claims process — from first notice of loss to payout — entirely within the customer's phone."

**Common mistakes:**
- More than two sentences
- Hype adjectives ("revolutionary", "best-in-class")
- Category that triggers wrong assumptions

### Stage 5: Value Themes with Detail

**Purpose:** Show HOW you deliver the perfect world. One section per value theme.

**Structure per theme:**
1. Theme name (from customer's perspective)
2. What it means for the customer
3. How it works (the unique attributes underneath)
4. Mapping to the "perfect world" from Stage 3

**Example (Theme: "Claims resolved in minutes, not days"):**
> "Our AI damage assessment engine analyzes uploaded photos in real-time, comparing against 2.3 million historical claims. Within 60 seconds, it generates a repair estimate accurate to within 5% of adjuster assessment. This means customers get an offer while they're still in the app — no waiting for a human review."

### Stage 6: Proof Points

**Purpose:** Remove doubt. Show evidence.

**Structure:**
- Per value theme: 1-2 proof points
- Handle top 2-3 objections
- Social proof (customer logos, quotes, case studies)

**Types of acceptable proof:**
| Type | Strength |
|------|----------|
| Named customer case study with metrics | Strongest |
| Customer quote (attributed) | Strong |
| Third-party review or award | Strong |
| Aggregate metrics ("average customer sees X") | Medium |
| Demo-able capability | Medium |
| Claimed capability without evidence | Unacceptable |

### Stage 7: Next Step / CTA

**Purpose:** Create momentum. One clear action.

**Match CTA to sales motion:**
| Motion | CTA |
|--------|-----|
| Self-serve / PLG | Free trial, sandbox, or "start for free" |
| SMB sales-led | Live demo or guided trial |
| Mid-market | Discovery call or technical workshop |
| Enterprise | Executive briefing or proof-of-concept |

## Story-on-a-Page Template

```
PROBLEM: [Target customers] face [specific challenge] because [root cause].

GAP: Today's solutions — [Alt A], [Alt B] — handle [what they do] but fail at [specific gap].

VISION: Imagine [ideal outcome 1], [ideal outcome 2], without [current pain].

PRODUCT: [Name] is a [category] that [unique mechanism].

VALUE:
• Theme 1: [Benefit] because [attribute]. Proof: [evidence].
• Theme 2: [Benefit] because [attribute]. Proof: [evidence].

PROOF: [Top customer], [metric], [third-party validation].

NEXT: [Specific CTA].
```

## Post-Positioning Cascade

After the sales story, positioning should flow to:

| Priority | Area | What Changes |
|----------|------|-------------|
| 1 | Master messaging doc | All campaigns and materials start from this baseline |
| 2 | Sales enablement | Demo scripts, objection handling, competitive battle cards |
| 3 | Website / landing pages | Hero copy, value props, social proof |
| 4 | Product roadmap | Feature priorities align with new market frame |
| 5 | Pricing | Align with category price expectations |
| 6 | Customer Success | Onboarding flow, expansion strategy |

## Positioning Tracking

Review triggers (check every 6 months):
- New credible competitor enters market
- Government regulation changes
- Economic climate shift
- New technology changes what's possible
- Customer attitude/preference shift

If repositioning needed: restart at Step 4 (competitive alternatives) and cascade forward.
