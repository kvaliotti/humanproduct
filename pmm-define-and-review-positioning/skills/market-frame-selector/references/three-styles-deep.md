# Three Positioning Styles: Deep Reference

## Style 1: Head to Head — Win an Existing Market

### How It Works
Accept the current market definition and buying criteria. Compete directly. Claim to be the best choice for most or all customers in the category.

### The Work Required

**If you're the leader:**
- Reinforce current buying criteria (they favor you)
- Defend against competitors trying to shift criteria
- Invest in category growth (rising tide lifts your boat most)

**If you're the challenger:**
- Prove you beat the leader on their own buying criteria
- Need HARD evidence — not just "we think we're better"
- Must be willing to invest heavily in awareness and credibility

### Risk Profile
- **Medium risk:** Buying criteria are known, market exists, demand exists
- **Key risk:** Incumbents have resources to respond. Fighting on their turf.

### When It Fails
- Small company tries to out-market an incumbent without clear superiority
- "We're like Salesforce but better" — without specific, provable claims about HOW

### Case Study
Database company repositioned as "data warehouse" — entered an existing category with demand but no clear leader. Immediately had context that prospects understood. Raised prices, changed roadmap.

## Style 2: Big Fish, Small Pond — Win a Subsegment

### How It Works
Take an existing market category. Carve off a piece where the rules are slightly different — just enough for your product to have a decisive edge over the category leader. You're not claiming to be the best CRM; you're claiming to be the best CRM *for investment banks*.

### The Work Required
1. **Educate the subsegment** on why general solutions fail them specifically
2. **Prove basic category fit** — you must meet table-stakes requirements ("yes, we're a real CRM")
3. **Quantify the value gap** — show the specific delta between leader's generic solution and your purpose-built one
4. **Dominate the subsegment** before expanding

### Advantages
- **Easier to target and reach:** Smaller, more homogeneous group. Know where they hang out, what they read.
- **Traction accelerates:** Each win directly applies to the next prospect in the subsegment. "Other investment banks use this" is powerful.
- **Word-of-mouth spreads naturally:** Tight communities talk. One happy customer → many referrals.
- **Expandable:** Win the pond, then take the next pond. Eventually, you're a lake.

### Risk Profile
- **Lowest risk for startups:** Existing demand, proven need, defensible niche
- **Key risk:** Subsegment too small, or leader notices and builds a subsegment-specific offering

### When It Fails
- Subsegment doesn't have meaningfully different needs from the broader market
- Subsegment is too small to sustain the business
- Leader can trivially add features to serve the subsegment

### Decision Criteria
All four must be true:
1. Category has a well-defined leader you can't beat head-on
2. Definable customer groups have needs the leader serves poorly
3. You can serve those needs meaningfully better
4. The subsegment is large enough to meet near-term business goals

### Case Study: Janna Systems
- Couldn't compete with Siebel ($2B revenue) in general enterprise CRM
- Repositioned as "CRM for investment banks"
- Investment banks needed relationship tracking, deal flow management — Siebel was generic
- Revenue: $2M → $70M in 18 months
- Acquired for $1.7B

### Expansion Path
1. Win the initial subsegment (investment banks)
2. Expand to adjacent subsegments (asset management, private equity)
3. Move upmarket or downmarket within the vertical
4. Eventually compete for the broader category from a position of strength

## Style 3: Create a New Game — Win a Market You Create

### How It Works
Define an entirely new market category. You set the boundaries, define what's in and what's out, establish the purchase criteria, and position yourself as the founding leader.

### The Work Required
1. **Prove the category deserves to exist:** Why is this problem unique? Why don't existing categories address it?
2. **Define evaluation criteria:** What should buyers look for? (Design criteria around your strengths)
3. **Establish leadership:** Be the definitive voice before fast-followers arrive
4. **Sell the problem first:** Prospects don't know the category exists. You must educate before you sell.
5. **Invest in ecosystem:** Content, events, analyst education, community

### Rewards (if successful)
- You define the market around your strengths — competitors must play by YOUR rules
- First-mover advantage in customer perception
- Once established, hard to dislodge (category = your brand)
- Premium pricing justified by category leadership

### Risks
- **Most expensive style:** Massive investment in education, content, events
- **Slowest payoff:** 2-5 years to establish a category
- **Biggest competitor risk:** You build the category, a larger player steals leadership
- **Execution risk:** Category may not take hold if market isn't ready

### When It Fails
- Product is really a feature of an existing category (not genuinely new)
- Market isn't ready for a new category (no enabling change)
- Larger competitor enters and outspends you on category education
- Team mistakes "cool technology" for "category-worthy problem"

### Decision Criteria
ALL must be true:
1. Evaluated every existing category — none makes your strengths obvious
2. Massive enabling change supports a new category (technology, regulation, economic)
3. Product is demonstrably, inarguably new
4. Have resources AND patience for long-term education investment

### Case Study: Eloqua
- Coined "marketing automation"
- No existing category (email marketing too narrow, CRM too broad) made their strengths obvious
- Revenue: $12M (2006) → $96M (2012)
- IPO, then acquired by Oracle for $870M
- **But:** This took 6+ years and massive category investment

### Warning Signs You're Creating When You Should Be Carving
- "No one else does what we do" — are you sure? Or are you in a subcategory of something that exists?
- "We need to educate the market" — is that because the category is new, or because your positioning is unclear?
- "We're beyond [existing category]" — what specifically makes you beyond it?
