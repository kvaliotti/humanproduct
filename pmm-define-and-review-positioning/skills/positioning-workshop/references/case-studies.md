# Positioning Case Studies

Cite these when the user's situation parallels one. Makes the framework concrete.

## Database → Data Warehouse (Repositioning into an Adjacent Category)

**Situation:** Small database company positioned as "database." Couldn't compete with Oracle. Prospects didn't listen.

**Turning point:** Customer said: "You're not a database, you're a data warehouse."

**Action:** Repositioned as data warehouse — a category with demand but no dominant leader.

**Result:** Prospects stopped comparing to Oracle. Started buying. Changed product roadmap, pricing, and company self-concept.

**When to cite:** User is stuck in a category dominated by a giant. There may be an adjacent category where they'd be the leader.

## Janna Systems: CRM → CRM for Investment Banks (Big Fish, Small Pond)

**Situation:** Couldn't compete with Siebel ($2B revenue) in general enterprise CRM. Feature-for-feature comparison always lost.

**Action:** Repositioned as "CRM for investment banks." Same core product, different frame. Investment banks had unique needs (relationship tracking, deal flow) that Siebel served poorly.

**Result:** Revenue grew from $2M to $70M in 18 months. Acquired for $1.7B.

**When to cite:** User has a solid product but can't compete head-on with category leader. A definable subsegment exists with unmet needs.

## Arm & Hammer: Baking Soda → Deodorizer (Letting Go of Baggage)

**Situation:** Iconic baking soda brand since 1846. Packaged food rose in the 1970s, home baking declined. Sales flatlined.

**Action:** Let go of "we are a baking product." Repositioned as refrigerator deodorizer, then extended to cat litter, underarm deodorant, and more.

**Result:** Revenue grew from $16M (1969) to $318M (1987).

**When to cite:** User is clinging to original product identity even though the market has shifted. Willingness to let go of history can unlock massive growth.

## Clearpath Robotics → OTTO Motors (Reframing the Category)

**Situation:** Built intelligent mobile robots for manufacturing. Positioned as "robots." But "robot" triggered assumptions of stationary, repetitive-task machines — not what they built.

**Action:** Reframed as "self-driving vehicles for industrial uses." Created OTTO Motors division. Added headlights and taillights to reinforce vehicle metaphor.

**Result:** Accelerated sales, $30M funding round, Edison Award.

**When to cite:** User's current category triggers wrong assumptions about what the product does. A different frame might make strengths obvious.

## Eloqua: Created "Marketing Automation" (New Category Creation)

**Situation:** Product didn't fit neatly into any existing category. Existing categories (email marketing, CRM) either understated capabilities or triggered wrong assumptions.

**Action:** Coined "marketing automation" as a new category. Invested heavily in category education — conferences, content, analyst relations.

**Result:** Revenue grew from $12M (2006) to $96M (2012). IPO. Acquired by Oracle for $870M.

**When to cite:** User's product is genuinely new and no existing category makes strengths obvious. BUT: warn that category creation is the most expensive and risky style. Eloqua had resources and patience most startups don't.

## Wave Accounting → Wave Financial Services (Multi-Product Repositioning)

**Situation:** Started as "Wave Accounting" — free cloud accounting for small businesses. Grew to 500K+ businesses, but advertising revenue model was insufficient.

**Insight:** Customers wanted digital invoicing, payments, payroll — not just accounting. "Accounting" in the name narrowed perception.

**Action:** Dropped "Accounting" from name → "Wave" → positioned as "financial software." Made additional products (payroll, payments, credit) feel natural.

**Result:** Grew to 4M+ customers, $200B+ tracked income/expenses.

**When to cite:** User has expanded beyond original product scope but positioning still reflects the original narrow frame.

## Redgate Software + DevOps (Layering a Trend)

**Situation:** Leader in database tools with 800K+ users. Business was great but not seen as strategic/urgent purchase.

**Action:** Didn't need a new category — they led theirs. Layered "database DevOps" trend onto existing positioning. Created content on databases' role in DevOps transformation.

**Result:** 100% increase in inbound leads. Dramatic increase in multi-product purchases.

**When to cite:** User has strong positioning in an established category but wants to add urgency. Trend layering (not trend replacing) is the right move.
