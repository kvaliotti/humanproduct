# The 10-Step Positioning Process (Detailed Reference)

## Sequential Dependency

```
Competitive Alternatives → Unique Attributes → Value → Target Customers → Market Category → (Trends)
```

Each component depends on the previous. Starting in the wrong place produces positioning that doesn't connect with how customers actually evaluate solutions.

## Pre-Work

### Step 1: Identify Best-Fit Customers

**Why start here:** The 5+1 components are interdependent and circular. Starting with happiest customers breaks the circle — they reveal value, fit, and competitive framing simultaneously.

**Best customer signals:**
- Understood product quickly
- Bought quickly
- Became raving fans and referrers
- Act as references
- Represent the type of customer you want more of

**Decision rules:**
| Situation | Action |
|---|---|
| Enough happy customers to see a pattern | Proceed with exercise |
| No happy customers yet | Keep positioning loose, test with diverse prospects, wait for patterns |
| Want to start with features, not customers | Resist — customer-facing positioning must center on customer frame |
| Want to use investor positioning for customers | Don't. Investors buy future vision; customers buy immediate value |

**Multi-product positioning:**
| Company Type | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| Single product | Position product and company as same thing |
| Multi-product, one revenue driver | Position that product first |
| Multi-product bundles | Position company first, then individual products |

### Step 2: Form a Positioning Team

**Must lead:** Business owner (CEO/founder for startups; division/BU leader for larger)

**Must represent (1-2 senior per function):**
| Function | Why | Unique Perspective |
|---|---|---|
| Marketing | Messaging, targeting, campaigns | How prospects discover and perceive |
| Sales/BD | Segments, account strategy | How prospects evaluate and decide |
| Customer Success | Onboarding, expansion | How customers experience value post-purchase |
| Product/Dev | Roadmap, prioritization | Differentiators and technical possibilities |

**Key:** Not a marketing exercise. Buy-in requires involvement. Keep to 3-12 people.

**Facilitation:** External facilitator strongly recommended — challenges sacred cows, ensures all voices heard.

### Step 3: Align Vocabulary, Drop Baggage

**Pre-work alignment on:**
1. What positioning means and why it matters
2. Which components make up a position
3. How market maturity affects positioning style

**Positioning baggage:** Unconscious assumptions from product conception that persist even when product/market changed.

**Surfacing questions:**
- Is current market the one identified at product launch?
- Using same terminology from launch?
- Do founders frame the product differently than newer employees?

**Method:** Call out history, get explicit agreement to suspend assumptions, be open to product being multiple things to multiple buyers.

## Core Analysis

### Steps 4-6: Competitive Alternatives, Unique Attributes, Value Themes

**Canonical reference:** These three steps are the analytical core of positioning and have their own deep-dive skill: `${CLAUDE_PLUGIN_ROOT}/skills/competitive-alternatives/SKILL.md`. It carries the full elicitation sequence, the attribute-category taxonomy, and the "So what?" clustering method. Use it (standalone or as a sub-step) instead of reconstructing the method here. What follows is the one-line essence of each step for quick orientation.

**Step 4 — List True Competitive Alternatives.** Core question: "What would our best customers do if we didn't exist?" Alternative types: direct competitor, general-purpose tools, manual process, do nothing, combinations. Critical distinction: competitive alternatives ≠ competitors — alternatives are what customers actually USE today. Rank most to least common, group into 2-5 clusters.

**Step 5 — Isolate Unique Attributes.** Definition: something the product/company HAS or DOES (not value — that's Step 6). Explore beyond product features: delivery model, business model, expertise, partnerships, team composition. Provable only; opinion doesn't count. Cast a wide net, then narrow.

**Step 6 — Map Attributes to Value Themes.** Chain: Feature → Benefit → Value (customer goal). Group related value points into 1-4 clusters from the customer's perspective. Each theme needs proof or it gets demoted/cut.

### Step 7: Determine Who Cares Most

**Actionable segmentation:** Easily identifiable characteristics that make someone really care about your value.

**Method:**
1. For each value theme: "Who cares about this intensely and why?"
2. Identify characteristics (industry, workflow, tools, pain frequency, team structure)
3. Validate: can you build a prospect list?

**Narrowing test:** Can you hit near-term sales targets with best-fit only? Yes = stay narrow. No = broaden until viable.

**Segment viability:**
1. Big enough for business goals (short-term)
2. Has specific, unmet needs
3. Easily identifiable for prospect lists

## Market Framing

### Step 8: Market Frame and Style

**Three methods to find market options:**
- Abductive reasoning: what products typically have these features?
- Examine adjacent (especially growing) markets
- Ask customers (cautiously)

**Three styles:** See `${CLAUDE_PLUGIN_ROOT}/references/three-styles.md` for full detail (decision criteria, work required, case studies for each style). The `market-frame-selector` skill is the dedicated deep-dive for this step.

### Step 9: Trend (Optional)

**Three-circle test:** Your strengths + market context + trend relevant to customers. All three must overlap.

**Rules:** Market first, trend second. Must link to product. Must be real to customers. Optional — better boring than baffling.

## Capture

### Step 10: Document and Share

Create one-page positioning canvas + detailed document. Living document, not filed and forgotten. Shows relationships between components.
