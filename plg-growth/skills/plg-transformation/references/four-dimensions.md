# Four Dimensions of PLG Transformation

Assessment framework for evaluating organizational readiness for product-led growth. Score each dimension 1-5, identify gaps, and prioritize transformation actions.

---

## Dimension 1: Strategic Alignment

**Core question:** Is PLG embedded in the company strategy, or is it a side project?

### Scoring Rubric

| Score | Description | Criteria |
|-------|------------|----------|
| 1 - Absent | PLG is not discussed at the strategic level | No executive awareness of PLG. Growth is assumed to come from sales/marketing only. No self-serve infrastructure investment. |
| 2 - Aware | PLG is discussed but not committed | Executives have heard of PLG. Some discussion about "should we offer a free tier?" No budget or team allocated. PLG is a "someday" initiative. |
| 3 - Piloting | PLG is an official initiative with limited scope | Executive sponsor exists. A small team is exploring PLG (free tier, self-serve signup). Budget allocated but limited. PLG is one of several growth channels. |
| 4 - Committed | PLG is a primary growth strategy | PLG is in the company strategy document. Significant investment: dedicated team, data infrastructure, self-serve product. Revenue driver tree used for planning. PLG metrics in board deck. |
| 5 - Native | PLG IS the growth model | Company was built product-led or has fully transformed. Self-serve is the primary revenue channel. Sales is product-led (assists product-qualified accounts). All teams think product-first. |

### Assessment Questions
- Is "product-led growth" or equivalent language in the company strategy?
- Is there an executive sponsor (VP+ level) who owns PLG outcomes?
- What % of engineering capacity is allocated to growth/self-serve vs enterprise features?
- What % of revenue comes from self-serve vs sales-assisted?
- Is there a revenue driver tree that the company operates against?
- When a new initiative is proposed, is "how does this affect PLG metrics?" a standard question?

### Common Gaps at Each Level
- **Score 1-2:** Need executive education. Build the business case: TAM accessible via self-serve, CAC comparison (self-serve vs sales), competitor PLG moves.
- **Score 2-3:** Need to move from discussion to commitment. Pilot with a small team, set a 90-day goal, measure and report to leadership.
- **Score 3-4:** Need to expand from pilot to strategy. Increase investment, set PLG-native goals across teams, integrate PLG into planning cycle.
- **Score 4-5:** Need cultural embedding. PLG thinking becomes default, not special. New hires onboarded into PLG philosophy.

## Dimension 2: Goal Setting

**Core question:** Are goals designed for product-led execution, or are they inherited from a sales-led model?

### Scoring Rubric

| Score | Description | Criteria |
|-------|------------|----------|
| 1 - Revenue only | Goals are top-line revenue targets | Only metric: ARR/MRR target. No decomposition. Teams scramble to "make number" without understanding drivers. |
| 2 - Lagging metrics | Goals include some operational metrics | Revenue + churn rate + customer count. All lagging indicators. No leading indicators that teams can influence in real-time. |
| 3 - Mixed metrics | Some PLG-native metrics exist | Activation rate, signup volume, or conversion rate tracked alongside revenue. But goals are not connected to the revenue tree. Teams may work on activation without knowing its revenue sensitivity. |
| 4 - PLG-native | Goals decomposed from revenue driver tree | Revenue driver tree exists. Team goals connected to specific tree branches. Leading indicators (activation, TTV, feature adoption) alongside lagging (MRR, churn). Sensitivity analysis informs where to focus. |
| 5 - Fully instrumented | Dynamic goals updated by tree sensitivity | Revenue tree is a live dashboard. Goals adjust based on where the biggest sensitivity lies. Teams own outcomes (metrics) not outputs (features). Quarterly goal-setting uses tree analysis to allocate focus. |

### Assessment Questions
- What are the current company-level goals? (List them)
- Are any of these product-usage metrics? (activation rate, feature adoption, TTV)
- Does a revenue driver tree exist? Who built it? Who uses it?
- Are team goals connected to specific branches of the tree?
- Do goals include leading indicators that teams can influence weekly?
- How are goals set? (Top-down revenue split? Bottom-up from tree analysis? Negotiated?)
- What happens when a team misses a goal? (Learning? Blame? Nothing?)

### Common Gaps at Each Level
- **Score 1-2:** Build a revenue driver tree. Decompose MRR into components. Show leadership that "grow revenue" is not actionable but "improve activation rate from 25% to 35%" is.
- **Score 2-3:** Add leading indicators. For each lagging goal, define the leading metric the team can influence weekly. Connect leading to lagging with a hypothesis.
- **Score 3-4:** Connect goals to the revenue tree. Run sensitivity analysis: which metric improvement drives the most MRR impact? Allocate goals accordingly.
- **Score 4-5:** Make the tree dynamic. Automate sensitivity analysis. Update team focus quarterly based on where the biggest opportunity shifts to.

## Dimension 3: Org Structure

**Core question:** Is the team organized to execute PLG, or is PLG bolted onto a structure designed for sales-led?

### Scoring Rubric

| Score | Description | Criteria |
|-------|------------|----------|
| 1 - No growth function | No dedicated growth team or thinking | Product teams build features. Marketing generates leads. Sales closes deals. Nobody owns the self-serve funnel end-to-end. |
| 2 - Growth is marketing | "Growth" sits in marketing, focused on top-of-funnel | Growth = demand gen + content + ads. No product involvement. No in-product growth levers. "Growth hacker" title without product authority. |
| 3 - Growth PM exists | One or a few PMs focused on growth, but embedded in product org | Growth PM(s) exist but share engineering with feature teams. No dedicated analyst. Compete for resources with enterprise feature requests. |
| 4 - Dedicated growth team | Cross-functional growth team with dedicated resources | Growth team has PM + designer + engineers + analyst. Clear ownership of AARMS metrics (or specific stages). Dedicated sprint capacity. Reports to product org. |
| 5 - Growth-native org | Multiple growth teams organized around revenue tree or AARMS | Teams own specific revenue components or AARMS stages. Each team is self-sufficient (PM, design, eng, data). Clear handoffs between teams. Growth team structure is the default, not the exception. |

### Assessment Questions
- Is there a dedicated growth team? What does it own?
- Does growth have dedicated engineering capacity, or does it compete with feature teams?
- Is there a dedicated growth analyst/data scientist?
- Does the design team have growth design experience (not just product/feature design)?
- Are teams cross-functional (PM + design + eng + data together) or functional silos?
- Who owns the self-serve funnel? (Nobody? Marketing? Product? A specific team?)
- How is capacity allocated between enterprise features and growth/self-serve work?

### Common Gaps at Each Level
- **Score 1-2:** Assign a growth PM. Even one PM dedicated to the self-serve funnel changes outcomes. Give them lightweight engineering support (even 1 engineer).
- **Score 2-3:** Move growth into product. Growth cannot be effective in marketing because it needs product authority. The growth PM needs engineering resources.
- **Score 3-4:** Dedicate resources. Growth PM needs a dedicated designer, 2-4 engineers, and an analyst. Stop competing for shared resources.
- **Score 4-5:** Expand and specialize. Add teams for specific AARMS stages or revenue components. Each team needs full cross-functional capability.

## Dimension 4: Org Culture

**Core question:** Does the culture support experimentation, data-driven decisions, user-centricity, and fast iteration?

### Scoring Rubric

| Score | Description | Criteria |
|-------|------------|----------|
| 1 - Feature factory | Ship features, hope they work | No experiments. No metrics per feature. Success = launch, not impact. Roadmap driven by sales requests or HiPPO (highest paid person's opinion). |
| 2 - Metrics-aware | Metrics exist but do not drive decisions | Dashboards exist but are rarely checked. Decisions are still gut-driven or stakeholder-driven. Occasional A/B test, but not habitual. |
| 3 - Data-informed | Data is one input to decisions | Regular dashboard reviews. Some experiments run (2-5/quarter). PM decisions reference data. But data can be overruled by opinion without explicit rationale. |
| 4 - Experiment-driven | Experiments are the primary decision mechanism | 10+ experiments per quarter. Hypothesis-driven development. Failed experiments celebrated for learnings. Data overrules opinion. Feature launches include success metrics. |
| 5 - Learning machine | Continuous experimentation with organizational learning | 20+ experiments per quarter. Every team runs experiments. Experiment results shared cross-functionally. Experiment velocity is a meta-metric. Failures are publicly shared and analyzed. The org learns faster than competitors. |

### Assessment Questions
- How many experiments did the team run last quarter? (0? 1-5? 5-10? 10+?)
- When a decision is made, what % of the time is data the primary input vs opinion?
- How often does the team talk to users? (Never? Quarterly? Monthly? Weekly?)
- When an experiment fails, what happens? (Blame? Nothing? Learning shared?)
- How fast does the team iterate? (Ship something new every day? Week? Month? Quarter?)
- Is the roadmap driven by data/experiments or by stakeholder requests?
- Does leadership model data-driven behavior? (Do they ask for data, or make pronouncements?)

### Common Gaps at Each Level
- **Score 1-2:** Start with one experiment. Just one. Show the team what it looks like to form a hypothesis, test it, and learn. Make the result visible to leadership.
- **Score 2-3:** Build the habit. Set a goal: 5 experiments this quarter. Create a simple experiment template. Review results in team meetings.
- **Score 3-4:** Remove barriers. Invest in experimentation infrastructure (feature flags, statistical analysis). Celebrate failed experiments. Make experiment review a ritual.
- **Score 4-5:** Scale the culture. Share experiment results cross-functionally. New hire onboarding includes experiment culture. Track experiment velocity as a meta-metric.

## Overall Assessment

### Composite View

Plot the four dimensions on a radar chart (or list scores):

```
Strategic Alignment:  [1-5]
Goal Setting:         [1-5]
Org Structure:        [1-5]
Org Culture:          [1-5]
Average:              [X.X]
```

### Interpretation

| Average Score | PLG Maturity Level | Implication |
|--------------|-------------------|-------------|
| 1.0-1.9 | Not PLG | Major transformation needed. Start with education and a pilot. |
| 2.0-2.9 | PLG-curious | Foundations need building. Focus on strategic alignment and a dedicated growth PM. |
| 3.0-3.9 | PLG-emerging | Basics in place. Focus on dedicated resources, experimentation culture, and PLG-native goals. |
| 4.0-4.9 | PLG-executing | Strong foundation. Optimize: scale teams, deepen experimentation, refine processes. |
| 5.0 | PLG-native | Best-in-class. Maintain and evolve. Focus on speed and learning velocity. |

### Prioritization Logic

Dimensions have dependencies:
1. **Strategic Alignment** must be >= 3 before other dimensions can sustainably improve. Without executive buy-in, growth teams get disbanded, budgets cut.
2. **Goal Setting** must be >= 3 for Org Structure to be effective. Without PLG-native goals, growth teams optimize for the wrong things.
3. **Org Structure** and **Org Culture** can improve in parallel, but both need the foundation of alignment and goals.

**Priority order when multiple dimensions are low:**
1. Strategic Alignment (prerequisite for everything)
2. Goal Setting (enables correct prioritization)
3. Org Structure (enables execution capacity)
4. Org Culture (enables execution quality)

### Hackman's 5 Factors for Team Effectiveness

When assessing org structure and culture, check against Hackman's research on effective teams:

| Factor | PLG Application | Assessment Question |
|--------|----------------|-------------------|
| Real Team | Growth team has clear membership and boundaries | Is it clear who is on the growth team? Do they have shared identity? |
| Compelling Direction | Team has a clear, challenging, consequential goal | Does the team have a metric they own that matters to the business? |
| Facilitating Structure | Right mix of skills, clear norms, manageable size | Does the team have PM + design + eng + data? Is it < 10 people? |
| Supportive Environment | Resources, information, training available | Does the team have dedicated capacity, data access, experimentation tools? |
| Coaching | Team receives feedback and guidance | Does someone coach the team on process, strategy, and experimentation? |
