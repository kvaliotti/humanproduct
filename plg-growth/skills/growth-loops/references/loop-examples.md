# Growth Loop Case Studies

Detailed breakdowns of real-world growth loops. For each: mechanism, metrics, what made it work, and limitations.

---

## 1. Calendly — Exposure Viral Loop

### Mechanism
Every meeting link Calendly users send is a product demo. Recipients experience the scheduling flow, see the Calendly brand, and are prompted to create their own.

### The Loop
```
User creates scheduling link
    → Sends link to meeting participant(s)
        → Participant views scheduling page (sees Calendly in action)
            → Participant books meeting (experiences the value)
                → Post-booking CTA: "Create your own Calendly"
                    → Participant signs up
                        → New user creates their own scheduling link
                            → Loop repeats
```

### Key Metrics
- **k-factor:** ~0.15 per month
- **Cycle time:** 2-5 days (time between scheduling events)
- **Exposure frequency:** Each user sends 3-10 scheduling links per week
- **Post-booking signup conversion:** ~5-8% of meeting participants eventually sign up

### What Made It Work
1. **Unavoidable exposure:** The product IS the delivery mechanism. You cannot use Calendly without exposing non-users to it.
2. **Value is immediately obvious:** The recipient experiences the pain-free scheduling flow. No explanation needed.
3. **Professional context:** Links are shared in professional settings where the recipient is likely a similar professional who also needs scheduling.
4. **Zero cost to share:** Users do not need to "decide" to share — sharing IS using the product.
5. **Repeat exposure:** Frequent schedulers send many links, creating multiple impressions on the same recipients.

### Limitations
- **Low per-interaction conversion:** Most meeting participants just book and leave. Only a small % sign up.
- **Addressable market constraint:** Once most professionals in a network use Calendly, the loop saturates.
- **Free tier cannibalization:** The free product is good enough for many users, so conversion to paid is the challenge (not adoption).
- **Competition visibility:** Competing tools (Cal.com, SavvyCal) can replicate the same exposure mechanism.

### Lessons for Other Products
- If your product's output is sent to non-users, you have an exposure viral opportunity
- The conversion step (CTA after booking) is the biggest optimization lever
- Professional tools shared in professional contexts have higher trust signals

## 2. Figma — Collaboration Viral Loop

### Mechanism
Design is inherently collaborative. Designers share files with stakeholders, developers, and other designers — all of whom need Figma accounts to participate meaningfully.

### The Loop
```
Designer creates a design in Figma
    → Shares file with teammates (developers, PMs, other designers)
        → Teammates create Figma accounts to view/comment
            → Some teammates start creating their own designs
                → New designers share with THEIR stakeholders
                    → Loop repeats across teams and companies
```

### Key Metrics
- **k-factor:** ~0.3 per month (high for B2B)
- **Cycle time:** 1-3 weeks (project cycle time)
- **Average invites per designer:** 5-8 collaborators in first month
- **Invitee-to-active-user rate:** ~40% become active (comment, iterate, create)
- **Cross-team spread rate:** ~15% of accounts spread to a new team/department within 6 months

### What Made It Work
1. **Collaboration is required:** Design review, developer handoff, and PM feedback all require account access. The loop is "mandatory."
2. **Browser-based:** No installation required. Link opens directly in browser. Zero friction for new users.
3. **Immediate value for viewers:** Even non-designers get value (viewing, commenting) without learning design tools.
4. **Multi-role loop:** Designers invite developers and PMs, who have their own networks in other companies.
5. **Free tier for viewers:** Generous free tier means no cost barrier for invitees. Figma captured developers and PMs who would never pay for a design tool.

### Limitations
- **Slow cycle time:** Design projects take weeks, so the loop compounds slowly.
- **Category-specific:** Only works because design is collaborative. Not transferable to solo-use products.
- **Enterprise procurement:** Large organizations eventually standardize, which can either accelerate (Figma wins the org) or block (competitor wins the org) the loop.

### Lessons for Other Products
- Collaboration viral loops have the highest k-factor but slowest cycle time
- Make the first experience for invitees zero-friction (browser-based, no install, immediate value)
- Multi-role products spread faster because each role has a different network

## 3. Quora — Content SEO Loop

### Mechanism
Users write answers to questions. Answers are indexed by Google. Searchers find answers, visit Quora, and some become contributors who write more answers.

### The Loop
```
User writes an answer to a question
    → Answer is indexed by Google
        → Searcher finds Quora result for their query
            → Searcher reads the answer on Quora
                → Some searchers sign up
                    → Some new users write their own answers
                        → New answers are indexed → Loop repeats
```

### Key Metrics
- **Organic search traffic:** 60%+ of all Quora traffic comes from Google
- **Content volume:** Millions of questions and answers
- **Contributor rate:** ~1-3% of new users write an answer within 30 days
- **Index rate:** ~70% of answers rank for at least one search query
- **Cycle time:** Weeks to months (SEO indexing and ranking takes time)

### What Made It Work
1. **Long-tail SEO coverage:** Every question targets a specific long-tail search query. Millions of questions = millions of ranking opportunities.
2. **User-generated content:** Content creation cost is zero for Quora — users create the content.
3. **Evergreen content:** Many answers remain relevant for years, accumulating traffic over time.
4. **Question-answer format matches search intent:** People search questions; Quora answers questions. Perfect format match.
5. **Quality signals:** Upvotes, author credentials, and answer length help both users and Google assess quality.

### Limitations
- **Low contributor rate:** The 1% rule means most users consume but never create. The loop depends on a tiny fraction of users completing it.
- **SEO vulnerability:** Google algorithm changes can significantly impact traffic (and did, with featured snippets and AI answers).
- **Quality control:** As scale increases, answer quality dilutes, which can hurt SEO rankings.
- **Long cycle time:** SEO takes months to compound, making this a slow-burn loop.

### Lessons for Other Products
- Content SEO loops work when users naturally create indexable content
- The contribution rate is always the bottleneck — invest in making content creation easy
- Diversify distribution (social + SEO) to reduce single-channel dependency

## 4. Linktree — Exposure Viral Loop

### Mechanism
Every Linktree profile is a permanent advertisement on social media bios. Every visitor to a creator's profile sees Linktree branding and can create their own.

### The Loop
```
Creator sets up Linktree as their bio link
    → Creator's followers/visitors click the bio link
        → Visitors see the Linktree page (with branding)
            → Some visitors create their own Linktree
                → New creators put Linktree in their bio
                    → Their followers see it → Loop repeats
```

### Key Metrics
- **k-factor:** ~0.05 per month (low per interaction, but extremely high frequency)
- **Cycle time:** Very short (hours to days — as fast as someone sees a bio and decides to create one)
- **Exposure frequency:** Extremely high. A bio link is visible on every profile visit, every day.
- **Aggregate growth rate:** Very high despite low k, because exposure volume is enormous

### What Made It Work
1. **Permanent placement:** A bio link is always visible, unlike a one-time share. It accumulates impressions indefinitely.
2. **Platform-agnostic:** Works on Instagram, TikTok, Twitter, YouTube — wherever bios exist.
3. **Aspirational signaling:** Creators see other creators using Linktree and want to look professional.
4. **Zero friction:** Create a Linktree in minutes, paste the link in your bio. No technical skill required.
5. **Free tier covers the basic use case:** Free plan is genuinely useful, removing the cost barrier.

### Limitations
- **Commodity product:** Very easy to clone (competitors: Beacons, Koji, Tap.bio, Stan Store).
- **Low per-visitor conversion:** Most visitors just want to see the links, not create their own page.
- **Creator dependency:** Growth is tied to creator adoption. If creators switch to competitors, the loop breaks.

### Lessons for Other Products
- Products that become "permanent fixtures" in visible locations compound exposure over time
- Even very low k-factors compound meaningfully when exposure frequency is high enough
- Simplicity and free tier are essential for exposure viral loops — any friction kills the loop

## 5. Notion — Collaboration + Content Hybrid Loop

### Mechanism
Notion has TWO active loops: (1) collaboration viral within teams, and (2) content/template sharing externally.

### Loop 1: Collaboration Viral
```
User creates workspace
    → Invites teammates
        → Team collaborates on docs/databases
            → Team members invite their contacts in other departments
                → Cross-department spread → Loop repeats
```

### Loop 2: Content/Template Sharing
```
User creates a template or public page
    → Shares template link externally (Twitter, Reddit, community)
        → Non-user discovers template
            → Signs up for Notion to use the template
                → New user customizes and shares their own templates
                    → Loop repeats
```

### Key Metrics
- **Collaboration k-factor:** ~0.2 per month
- **Template/content k-factor:** ~0.05 per month (additive)
- **Combined effective k:** ~0.25 per month
- **Cycle time:** 1-4 weeks for collaboration, 1-2 weeks for templates
- **Cross-team spread:** ~20% of workspaces expand to a new team within 6 months

### What Made It Work
1. **Dual loop compounding:** Two loops running simultaneously amplify each other.
2. **Template ecosystem:** Community-created templates serve as both product demos and content marketing.
3. **Flexible product:** Notion serves many use cases (docs, databases, wikis, project management), creating more collaboration contexts.
4. **Bottom-up adoption:** Individual users adopt for personal use, then bring Notion to their team.
5. **Community-driven growth:** Active user community creates tutorials, templates, and evangelism.

### Limitations
- **Complexity barrier:** New users often feel overwhelmed by Notion's flexibility. Activation is the bottleneck.
- **Slow enterprise adoption:** Bottom-up adoption hits procurement barriers in large orgs.
- **Template quality variance:** Community templates vary in quality, creating inconsistent first experiences.

### Lessons for Other Products
- Multiple loops compound: even two weak loops together can create strong growth
- Community and templates can serve as a content loop even for non-content products
- Bottom-up + collaboration + content is a powerful PLG growth trifecta
