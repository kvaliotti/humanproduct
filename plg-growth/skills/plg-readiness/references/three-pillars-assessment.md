# Three Pillars of PLG — Deep Assessment Guide

## Overview

The 3 pillars are the foundational design principles that separate PLG products from products that merely have a free tier. Assess each pillar and its sub-dimensions to identify where the product is strong and where it needs work.

---

## Pillar 1: Design for the End User

The product must be built for the person who uses it, not just the person who buys it.

### Sub-dimension 1.1: Easy to Understand

**Assessment questions:**
- Can a first-time visitor understand what the product does from the homepage in <10 seconds?
- Is the value proposition clear without jargon or buzzwords?
- Can you explain the product in one sentence that a non-expert understands?
- Does the product category help or hurt understanding? (Known category = easier; new category = harder)

**Scoring:**
| Score | Indicator |
|-------|-----------|
| 5 | Instant clarity. Category is known, positioning is sharp, user gets it immediately. |
| 4 | Clear with brief explanation. Landing page communicates well. |
| 3 | Requires some context. User needs to read docs or watch a video. |
| 2 | Confusing to most. Multiple features, unclear primary use case. |
| 1 | Requires a sales call to understand. Abstract enterprise value prop. |

**Improvement actions:**
- Simplify landing page to one clear use case
- Use "show don't tell" — interactive demo, video, or example
- Pick one ICP and speak directly to their problem
- Remove feature lists, lead with outcome

### Sub-dimension 1.2: Easy to Use

**Assessment questions:**
- Can a new user complete the core workflow on their first visit without training?
- What is the learning curve? (Minutes? Hours? Days?)
- How much of the product is discoverable vs. requires documentation?
- Is the UX consistent with patterns users already know?
- What is the support ticket rate for new users?

**Scoring:**
| Score | Indicator |
|-------|-----------|
| 5 | Zero learning curve. Familiar patterns, intuitive UI, guided first run. |
| 4 | Minimal learning. Brief onboarding flow, user is productive quickly. |
| 3 | Moderate learning curve. Needs tutorial or onboarding sequence. |
| 2 | Steep learning curve. Requires training, certification, or dedicated onboarding. |
| 1 | Expert-only. Requires implementation partner or professional services. |

**Improvement actions:**
- Implement progressive disclosure (show basics first, advanced later)
- Add interactive onboarding / product tour for first-time users
- Provide templates and presets for common use cases
- Reduce time-to-first-action (what can user DO in first 60 seconds?)

### Sub-dimension 1.3: Easy to Purchase

**Assessment questions:**
- Can a user upgrade to paid without talking to sales?
- Is pricing transparent and published on the website?
- Can they pay with a credit card?
- Is the upgrade path clear from within the product?
- How many approval steps are typically needed?

**Scoring:**
| Score | Indicator |
|-------|-----------|
| 5 | One-click upgrade in-product. Transparent pricing. No approval needed. |
| 4 | Self-serve purchase with transparent pricing. Might need manager approval for team plans. |
| 3 | Pricing visible but complex. Self-serve for individuals, sales-assist for teams. |
| 2 | "Contact sales" for pricing. Self-serve only for lowest tier. |
| 1 | All purchasing through sales. Custom pricing, contracts, procurement. |

## Pillar 2: Deliver Value Before You Capture Value

The product must demonstrate its worth before asking users to pay.

### Sub-dimension 2.1: Activation & Aha Moment

**Assessment questions:**
- Is the aha moment clearly defined? (What specific experience makes users say "I need this"?)
- What % of signups reach the aha moment? (Activation rate)
- How many steps from signup to aha moment?
- Can the aha moment happen in a single session?

**Defining the aha moment — framework:**
The aha moment is when the user first experiences the core value proposition. It is NOT:
- Completing onboarding
- Creating an account
- Seeing a dashboard

It IS:
- The moment they get a result they could not get before
- The moment they save meaningful time or effort
- The moment they see data/insight that changes a decision

**Examples:**
| Product | Aha Moment |
|---------|-----------|
| Canva | First design looks professional |
| Slack | Team communication flows in real-time |
| Amplitude | First behavioral insight from their data |
| Grammarly | First writing error caught and fixed |
| Figma | First real-time collaboration on a design |

**Scoring:**
| Score | Indicator |
|-------|-----------|
| 5 | Aha moment in <5 minutes. >60% of signups reach it. |
| 4 | Aha moment in <1 hour. 40-60% of signups reach it. |
| 3 | Aha moment in <1 day. 20-40% of signups reach it. |
| 2 | Aha moment in days. 10-20% of signups reach it. |
| 1 | Aha moment in weeks, or <10% reach it, or aha moment is undefined. |

### Sub-dimension 2.2: Time to Value (TTV)

**Assessment questions:**
- How long from signup to first meaningful value delivered?
- What are the mandatory steps before value? (Can any be removed or deferred?)
- Can value be shown with sample/demo data while user's own data loads?
- Is TTV different for different user segments?

**TTV reduction strategies:**
1. Pre-populate with sample data or templates
2. Defer non-essential setup (progressive profiling)
3. Show value preview before requiring account creation
4. Auto-detect and configure based on user context
5. "Quick start" mode that skips advanced configuration

### Sub-dimension 2.3: Value-Capture Ratio

**Assessment questions:**
- How much of the core value is available for free?
- What is behind the paywall — essential features or power features?
- Does the free experience create desire for paid features (natural upgrade motivation)?
- Is the free tier generous enough to demonstrate value but limited enough to drive upgrades?

**The balance:**
- Too generous: users never upgrade (conversion problem)
- Too restrictive: users don't experience value (activation problem)
- Just right: users get hooked on value and hit a natural expansion point

## Pillar 3: Build Product with Go-To-Market Intent (TASE)

The product itself is the growth engine. Four dimensions: Traffic, Activation, Stickiness, Expansion.

### T — Traffic: Does the Product Generate Its Own Traffic?

**Assessment questions:**
- Do product outputs drive SEO? (Public pages, templates, content)
- Do users share product outputs? (Links, embeds, exports with branding)
- Does the product create inbound links? (Badges, widgets, integrations)
- Is there a marketplace or template gallery that attracts search traffic?
- Do API/integration partners drive traffic?

**Examples:**
- Canva: public design templates rank in search
- Notion: public pages indexed by Google
- Figma: community files drive awareness
- Typeform: branded survey links shared widely

### A — Activation: Is Onboarding a Growth Lever?

**Assessment questions:**
- Is onboarding designed to maximize aha-moment rate, or just to explain features?
- Are there onboarding experiments running?
- Is activation rate a team-level metric?
- Does onboarding adapt based on user segment/intent?

### S — Stickiness: Does Usage Build Habit and Switching Cost?

**Assessment questions:**
- Does the product accumulate data/content that makes it harder to leave?
- Is it part of daily/weekly workflow?
- How many integrations does the average active user connect?
- What is the cost (time, data, workflow disruption) of switching to a competitor?

### E — Expansion: Does Individual Use Become Team/Org Use?

**Assessment questions:**
- What % of accounts expand from 1 user to 2+?
- What is the average time from first user to second user on an account?
- Is there a natural "share with team" moment in the workflow?
- Does the product have team/workspace features?
- What is net revenue retention? (>110% = strong expansion engine)
