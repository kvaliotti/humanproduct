# Research Playbook

Purpose: ground the matrix in practitioner language, real substitutes, and actual workflow steps. Five to ten well-targeted searches beats twenty shallow ones.

## Search query patterns

Replace `[domain]` with the industry, `[role]` with the end-user persona, `[anchor]` with the anchor product.

### Discovering practitioner language and workflow

```
how do [role] do [main activity] workflow
[domain] process end to end
[role] daily workflow [domain]
[domain] playbook / guide / checklist
[anchor] alternatives
best [domain] tools [current year]
```

Aim to find practitioner blog posts, forum threads (Reddit, Hacker News, niche subreddits), "how we do X at [company]" posts, and industry op-eds. Avoid vendor marketing pages for language discovery — they bias toward their own categorization.

### Discovering substitutes and non-consumption

```
[anchor] vs spreadsheet / manual / agency
"we tried [anchor] then built our own"
DIY [main activity] [domain]
[domain] without [category of tools]
```

Forums and indie-hacker blogs are gold here. Substitutes tell you what the real baseline is.

### Discovering channels, methods, and platforms

```
[main activity] channels / platforms / methods
[role] tools comparison
[domain] category breakdown G2 / Capterra
```

G2 and Capterra category pages are useful for enumerating vendors and sub-categories, but treat them as inputs to filter, not truth. Their taxonomy is vendor-biased.

### Discovering the anchor's actual scope

If the user provided an anchor, fetch the anchor's:
- Homepage (the "what we do" page)
- Pricing page (reveals product modules)
- "Solutions" or "use cases" page (reveals claimed use cases)

Don't copy their framing — *resist* it. The anchor's marketing tells you what they sell, which is a subset of what users actually do.

## Budget

- 5-8 WebSearch calls is the sweet spot
- 1-3 WebFetch calls for anchor homepage and maybe 1-2 competitor or guide pages
- Stop when the last two queries aren't adding new vocabulary or workflow steps — diminishing returns are real

## What to capture

Keep a scratch list as research proceeds:

- **Verbs** — what practitioners say they *do* (e.g., "brief the creative team", "pull a keyword gap report")
- **Named steps** — workflow phases with recognized names ("creative refresh cycle", "bid optimization")
- **Channels** — platforms the work happens on
- **Methods** — how people approach a step (manually, tool, agency, automation)
- **Substitutes** — what people do instead of tools in the obvious category
- **Jargon** — terms that would be opaque to an outsider but natural to practitioners
- **Pain signals** — complaints, repeated frustrations (input for skill #2 later)

Drop the scratch list into the output's glossary and open-questions sections where relevant.

## Anti-patterns

- **Listing all vendors and calling it research.** Vendors are evidence, not the map.
- **Taking the anchor's taxonomy as given.** The whole point is to go broader.
- **Mixing global and regional context silently.** If workflows differ by geography (e.g., China app stores vs. western), call it out.
- **Relying on one source.** Triangulate — at least 2-3 sources for any structural claim.
